Article 1.4 - Exchange Rates and Your Investments

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You may never have traded a single US dollar, but if you own shares in a Tanzanian company or send money home from abroad, the exchange rate between the Tanzanian shilling (TZS) and other currencies directly affects your returns. This article explains how exchange rates work, why they move, and how they ripple through your portfolio — with practical, DSE-focused examples.

1. What is an exchange rate?

An exchange rate is simply the price of one country’s currency expressed in another. For example, if USD/TZS = 2,636, that means one US dollar buys 2,636 Tanzanian shillings. When the shilling weakens, the number goes up (more shillings per dollar). When the shilling strengthens, the number goes down.

2. What moves exchange rates?

In the long run, exchange rates reflect the relative strength of economies. In the short run, they are driven by:

Trade flows: When Tanzania exports more than it imports — gold, cashews, tourism — foreign currency flows in, strengthening the shilling. When imports exceed exports, the shilling weakens.

Interest rate differences: If the Bank of Tanzania offers higher interest rates than the US Federal Reserve, foreign investors may move money into Tanzanian bonds to capture the higher yield. That demand for shillings can strengthen the currency. If they pull money out, the shilling falls.

Market sentiment: Political stability, regulatory predictability, and global risk appetite all affect whether foreigners want to hold shilling-denominated assets.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we report the TZS/USD and TZS/GBP rates every day for a reason: they tell you the purchasing power of the shilling on the global stage.

3. How exchange rates hit different DSE stocks

The impact of a stronger or weaker shilling depends on whether a company is a net importer, a net exporter, or domestically focused.

Type of Company Effect of a Weaker Shilling Effect of a Stronger Shilling
Importers (manufacturers relying on imported raw materials, fuel distributors) Negative: Input costs rise, squeezing margins. Positive: Cheaper imports boost margins.
Exporters (gold, cashews, coffee, tourism) Positive: Foreign revenue translates to more shillings. Negative: Foreign revenue shrinks in shilling terms.
Domestic-focused companies (banks, telecoms, local retailers) Mixed: Inflation may rise from imported goods, but some companies can pass costs on. Mixed: Lower import costs may reduce inflation but could slow growth.
Bond investors Negative: Falling shilling erodes the value of interest payments for foreign investors, potentially driving them out. Positive: Rising shilling attracts foreign capital, pushing bond prices up.
Diaspora investors Beneficial: Your foreign currency converts to more shillings, increasing your buying power on the DSE. Less beneficial: Your foreign currency buys fewer shillings.

On the DSE, companies like TBL (which imports some inputs) and TOL (which imports fuel) are sensitive to exchange rate moves. Banks like CRDB and NMB are more insulated, but a prolonged currency crisis can still hurt their loan portfolios if their corporate clients struggle.

4. The exchange rate and inflation: the pass-through effect

There is a direct link between exchange rates and the inflation we discussed in Article 1.3. When the shilling weakens, imported goods — fuel, machinery, cooking oil — become more expensive. This pushes up the Consumer Price Index. The Bank of Tanzania then faces a dilemma: raise interest rates to fight inflation (which may slow the economy) or allow the shilling to float and hope inflation subsides.

For an investor, a sharp drop in the shilling can trigger a chain reaction: higher inflation → potential rate hikes → lower equity valuations → possible selling pressure on imported-input companies. This is exactly the type of macro-to-market logic we apply in the Daily DSE Wrap when interpreting foreign flow data.

5. Diaspora investing: making exchange rates work for you

If you are a Tanzanian living in the UK, Europe, or the USA, you are in a unique position: you earn in a hard currency and invest in a frontier market. Exchange rate movements can amplify your returns.

A weaker shilling works in your favour when buying in: your pounds or dollars convert to more shillings, allowing you to purchase more shares.

A stronger shilling helps when you eventually sell and repatriate funds: you get more foreign currency for your shillings.

This dual advantage means diaspora investors can systematically accumulate DSE shares when the shilling is weak and benefit from both capital appreciation and potential currency gains when they exit.

6. How to track exchange rates

The Bank of Tanzania website publishes daily exchange rates.

The DSE daily reports include TZS/USD, TZS/KE, and TZS/GBP rates — these are reproduced on our Current Prices page.

Global news: Watch for US Federal Reserve and Bank of England rate decisions, which can move capital flows in and out of frontier markets.

In our Daily DSE Wrap, we explicitly mention foreign participation percentages — buying and selling. When you see a spike in foreign selling, one possible driver is a shilling depreciation expectation. When foreign buying surges, it may reflect confidence in a stable currency.

Key Takeaway

The exchange rate is a transmission belt that carries global economic forces directly into your portfolio. By understanding how the shilling moves and why, you can position yourself to benefit from currency trends rather than being blindsided by them.

Put It Into Practice

Open today’s Daily DSE Wrap. Note the TZS/USD rate and the foreign investor activity. If you are a diaspora investor, calculate how many shares of your favourite stock you could buy today versus six months ago, given the exchange rate movement.
