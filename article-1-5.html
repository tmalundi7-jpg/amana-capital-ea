Article 1.5 - The Economic Cycle: When to Plant and When to Harvest

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Markets do not move in a straight line. Just as the seasons dictate when a farmer plants and when they harvest, the economy moves through a rhythm — expansion, peak, contraction, and recovery. This is the economic cycle. For an investor, understanding this cycle provides a map of where the economy is and which sectors are likely to thrive or struggle at each stage.

1. What is the economic cycle?

The economic cycle is the natural fluctuation of economic activity around a long-term growth trend. It has four phases:

Expansion: GDP is growing. Businesses are hiring, consumers are spending, credit is readily available, and stock markets tend to rise. This phase can last for years.

Peak: Growth reaches its maximum rate. The economy may be running at full capacity. Inflation often picks up, and the central bank may start raising interest rates. Stock markets become volatile.

Contraction (recession): GDP growth slows or turns negative. Companies cut back, unemployment rises, and stock markets usually decline.

Recovery: The economy starts growing again. Confidence returns, credit eases, and businesses begin to expand. Stock markets often rebound before the official statistics confirm the recovery.

These phases are not perfectly regular, but they have repeated throughout economic history in every country — including Tanzania.

2. How different sectors perform at each stage

Different industries have different sensitivities to the economy, so the cycle creates a rotating cast of leaders and laggards.

Phase Sectors That Tend to Perform Well Sectors That Tend to Lag
Early Expansion Financials (banks, insurance), consumer discretionary (brewers, media), industrials Defensive sectors (utilities, bonds)
Late Expansion / Peak Energy, materials (cement, metals), technology Interest-rate-sensitive sectors (real estate)
Contraction / Recession Consumer staples (food, beverages, telecoms), healthcare Cyclical sectors (financials, industrials, luxury goods)
Recovery Cyclicals (financials, consumer discretionary, real estate) Safe havens (government bonds lose some appeal)

On the DSE, the banking sector often leads in early expansion. Consumer goods companies like TBL benefit from rising disposable incomes. In a contraction, defensive stocks like VODA (telecoms) might hold up better because people keep using their phones regardless of the economy.

3. Where are we now? Reading the cycle in Tanzania

As of mid-2026, Tanzania’s economy has been in a sustained expansion, with GDP growth around 5–6%. The DSEI has climbed significantly from a year ago. The banking sector has been leading, and foreign selling has collapsed to minimal levels — a sign that local confidence is driving the market higher. This is consistent with a mid-expansion phase, where growth is steady but not yet overheated, and inflation remains within the Bank of Tanzania’s comfort zone.

The danger in a mid-expansion phase is complacency. The cycle always turns. The Bank of Tanzania will eventually need to raise rates if inflation threatens, or an external shock could slow the economy. Being a disciplined, long-horizon investor means preparing for the next phase even while enjoying the current one.

4. How to position your portfolio across the cycle

You cannot time the cycle perfectly, and trying to jump in and out of sectors usually backfires. Instead:

Stay diversified. Hold a mix of financials, industrials, consumer goods, and telecoms.

Re-balance annually. Trim sectors that have outperformed and add to underweight sectors.

Keep some cash or bond exposure. A reserve allows you to buy equities during a contraction, when prices are depressed.

Focus on companies, not macro forecasts. Well-managed companies with strong balance sheets can survive and emerge stronger from any downturn.

5. The Daily DSE Wrap as your cycle compass

Every trading day, our Market Pulse section gives you the key cycle indicators: equity turnover and deals, foreign flow, bond activity, and sector indices. By reading the Wrap with the economic cycle in mind, you move from observing numbers to understanding the deeper narrative they form.

Key Takeaway

The economic cycle is the farmer’s almanac for investors. You don’t need to predict the weather perfectly; you just need to know enough to plant the right seeds at the right time and to keep your harvest safe when conditions change.

Put It Into Practice

Look at today’s sector indices in the Daily DSE Wrap. Identify the best-performing and worst-performing sectors. Then, using the table in this article, consider which phase of the cycle that pattern suggests.
