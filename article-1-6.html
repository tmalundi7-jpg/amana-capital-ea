Article 1.6 - How Government Spending and National Debt Affect Markets

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Every year, the Tanzanian government collects taxes, receives foreign aid, and borrows money to spend on roads, schools, hospitals, and defence. The way it manages its finances — through fiscal policy — creates powerful currents that flow directly into the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange. This article explains those currents and teaches you how to read the budget as an investor.

1. Government spending: the fuel and the drag

When the government spends money on infrastructure — a new railway, a port expansion, an electrification project — it pours cash into the economy. Construction companies earn revenue; they hire workers; those workers spend wages. The initial spending multiplies through the economy. Companies that provide cement, steel, and logistics — think Tanga Cement (TCC), Portland Cement (TPCC), and TOL — often see their order books swell.

But government spending must be paid for. If the government borrows aggressively to finance that spending, it can crowd out private borrowers. Banks may prefer to lend to the government (via Treasury bonds) rather than to businesses, especially if bond yields are attractive. This is one reason we track bond turnover closely in the Daily DSE Wrap: large bond issuance can pull cash away from the equity market.

2. National debt: when borrowing becomes a concern

Tanzania’s national debt has grown in recent years, driven by development projects and pandemic-era borrowing. For an investor, the level of debt matters because:

High debt can lead to higher taxes on corporations and individuals, which reduces corporate earnings and disposable income.

High debt can weaken the shilling if foreign investors fear default or currency depreciation.

High debt can force the government to cut spending in other areas, hurting sectors that depend on public contracts.

However, not all debt is equal. Debt used to build productive assets — a port that boosts trade, a power plant that cuts electricity costs — can generate returns that more than cover the interest cost. As investors, we distinguish between productive debt and wasteful debt.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we note government bond auction results. When a bond is oversubscribed, it signals confidence in the government’s creditworthiness. When yields spike, it may signal nervousness about debt sustainability.

3. The budget cycle: dates every DSE investor should know

The Tanzanian fiscal year runs from July to June. The Minister of Finance presents the national budget in June, detailing spending plans, tax changes, and borrowing requirements. This speech is one of the most important events of the year for the stock market.

What to watch in the budget:

Corporate tax changes — directly affect listed company earnings.

Excise duties — changes on beer, cigarettes, fuel, and mobile services impact TBL, TCC, VODA, and others.

Infrastructure allocations — signal which sectors will benefit from government contracts.

Bond issuance plans — indicate how much the government will borrow, affecting bond yields and equity liquidity.

After the budget, the Daily DSE Wrap will analyse the implications sector by sector, so you don’t have to guess.

4. How to integrate fiscal policy into your investment process

Read the budget speech (or at least our summary in the Daily Wrap). Identify the sectors that will gain from spending increases and those that will suffer from tax hikes.

Track government bond yields. Rising yields may indicate that the government is borrowing more aggressively, which can compete with equities for investment capital.

Monitor the national debt-to-GDP ratio. As a rule of thumb, a ratio below 50% is comfortable; above 70% can become problematic. Tanzania’s ratio is currently manageable but worth watching.

5. The Daily DSE Wrap connection

Every major fiscal event — budget speech, bond auction, tax announcement — receives dedicated coverage in the Daily Wrap. When you read our analysis of why the bond market moved or why construction stocks rallied, the explanation often traces back to a fiscal policy decision made weeks or months earlier. Understanding fiscal policy gives you the “why” behind the “what.”

Key Takeaway

Infrastructure-heavy budgets are generally positive for industrial and construction stocks, but watch the funding side — heavy borrowing can push up interest rates and pressure bank lending margins. A disciplined investor reads the budget not as a political document, but as a roadmap for capital allocation.

Put It Into Practice

Next time you read the Daily DSE Wrap, check if any government-related news is mentioned. Ask yourself: how does this spending or borrowing plan affect the sectors I hold?

2.1 Supply, Demand, and the Business That Sells It

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

In Module 1, we explored the broad economy. Now we zoom in to the individual company. The most fundamental tool for understanding a business is the law of supply and demand — the same force that determines the price of onions in Kariakoo also determines the profits of TBL, VODA, and CRDB.

The Basics

Supply is how much producers are willing to sell at a given price. Higher prices usually mean more supply.

Demand is how much consumers are willing to buy. Lower prices boost demand.

Equilibrium is where supply meets demand — the market price.

For a business, the goal is to sell at a price well above the cost of production. The difference is profit.

Pricing Power Not all companies can raise prices without losing customers. A company with pricing power can increase prices steadily over time without significantly reducing demand. This power comes from:

Strong brand (TBL’s Safari Lager commands loyalty).

Few competitors (Tanzania’s cement industry has limited players, aiding TCC and TPCC).

High switching costs (a bank where a client holds a mortgage, salary account, and mobile banking is hard to leave).

Companies with pricing power are generally better investments because they protect margins during inflation and can reinvest profits for growth.

Demand Shifts Demand changes due to rising incomes, population growth, or new technology. As Tanzania’s middle class expands, demand for financial services, mobile data, and branded goods increases, benefiting CRDB, NMB, and VODA. When the government invests in infrastructure, demand for cement rises, boosting TCC and TPCC. In the Daily DSE Wrap, turnover and volume reveal where demand is flowing in real time.

Supply-Side Risks Even strong demand won’t help a company that can’t deliver. Drought, power outages, or import delays can crush margins. For example, TOL, which imports fuel, faces supply risks if global oil prices spike or the shilling weakens. Ask: Where do raw materials come from? How dependent is the company on imports? Does it have reliable power and water?

Daily DSE Wrap Connection Every volume spike and price movement is supply and demand at work. On 29 June 2026, NMB traded 228,782 shares (including a block trade) — that’s demand meeting supply at a mutually agreeable price near TZS 16,160. Sellers were not desperate and buyers were willing to pay.

2.2 How to Read a Company’s Financial Statements (Part 1 – The Income Statement)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Financial statements are the report card of a business. The first and most watched is the income statement (profit and loss statement). It answers: Is this company making money?

Anatomy of an Income Statement Every income statement follows this structure:

Revenue (Sales): Total money from selling goods or services.

– Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): Direct costs of production.

= Gross Profit

– Operating Expenses: Salaries, rent, marketing, depreciation.

= Operating Profit (EBIT)

– Interest Expense + Interest Income – Tax

= Net Profit

For a Tanzanian bank like CRDB, interest income from loans is revenue; interest paid on deposits is COGS; the difference is net interest income. After branch and staff costs, we get operating profit.

Margins: The True Test

Gross Margin = (Gross Profit / Revenue) × 100.

Operating Margin = (Operating Profit / Revenue) × 100.

Net Margin = (Net Profit / Revenue) × 100.

Compare margins over time and across competitors. A declining margin may signal rising costs, competition, or poor management. A stable or expanding margin is positive.

Real DSE Application When you read an annual report, ask: Is revenue growing? Are costs growing faster than revenue? Is net profit sustainable or boosted by one-off items? The Daily DSE Wrap often notes earnings results — for example, if NMB’s net interest margin improved due to lower funding costs, that’s the income statement at work.

2.3 How to Read a Company’s Financial Statements (Part 2 – The Balance Sheet)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

The balance sheet shows what a company owns and owes at a single point in time. It reveals hidden strengths or looming dangers the income statement alone misses.

The Fundamental Equation Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders’ Equity

Assets are what the company owns: cash, buildings, inventory, receivables.

Liabilities are what it owes: loans, supplier payables, bonds.

Shareholders’ Equity is the net worth left for owners after debts are paid.

Key Items

Cash and equivalents: Can the company survive a shock?

Accounts receivable: Money owed by customers. Fast-growing receivables may signal collection problems.

Inventory: Too much ties up cash; too little may lose sales.

Property, plant & equipment (PPE): Fixed assets. Heavy investment signals expansion but also depreciation.

Short-term debt: Must be manageable relative to cash and cash flow.

Long-term debt: Excessive debt can choke a company in a downturn.

Retained earnings: Cumulative profits not paid as dividends; show how much has been reinvested.

The Debt-to-Equity Ratio Total Liabilities / Shareholders’ Equity. A ratio above 2.0 is high for non-financial companies. Banks naturally have higher leverage. Check this ratio against peers and over time. For example, TCC and TPCC, as cement manufacturers, may carry significant debt for plant construction, but their debt should be serviceable from operating cash flow.

Working Capital Current Assets – Current Liabilities. Positive working capital means the company can meet short-term obligations. A worsening trend may signal collection or inventory problems. Watch for a sudden spike in receivables without a corresponding rise in revenue — that could mean customers are delaying payments, tying up cash.

2.4 The Cash Flow Statement: Where the Money Really Is

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. A company can report healthy net profit yet go bankrupt if it runs out of cash. The cash flow statement shows actual money movement.

Three Sections

Operating Activities: Cash from core business — sales, salaries, collections. Positive operating cash flow is essential.

Investing Activities: Cash spent on assets (new factories) or received from selling them. Negative investing cash flow may be okay if expansion is productive and funded by operations.

Financing Activities: Cash from issuing shares or loans, and cash paid as dividends or debt repayments.

Free Cash Flow (FCF) FCF = Operating Cash Flow – Capital Expenditures. FCF is the cash left after maintaining or expanding the asset base. It can pay dividends, reduce debt, or fund growth. Companies with strong, growing FCF are prized.

Real DSE Application A cement company might have strong operating cash flow but heavy investing cash flow if building a new plant. That’s fine if the expansion will generate future returns. But if operating cash flow is weak and it’s borrowing heavily, that’s a red flag. In the Daily DSE Wrap, dividend announcements reflect cash flow strength — like TBL’s TZS 818 per share dividend signalling ample FCF.

2.5 Competitive Moats: Why Some Companies Stay on Top

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Why can a brewery raise prices without losing customers? Why does one bank dominate lending? The answer is a competitive moat — a durable advantage that protects a company from rivals.

Types of Moats

Brand: Consumers trust and pay a premium. (TBL’s Safari Lager)

Cost advantage: Producing at lower cost through scale or superior technology. (TPCC’s local limestone reserves)

Network effect: Service becomes more valuable as more people use it. (VODA’s mobile money)

Regulatory licence: Limited competition through licensing. (Banking licences from BOT)

High switching costs: Customers find it expensive or inconvenient to leave. (Corporate clients with multiple accounts at CRDB)

Identifying a Moat Ask: Can the company raise prices without losing significant sales? Does it consistently earn higher returns on equity (ROE) than peers? Has its market share been stable or growing? Would a new competitor struggle to replicate what it does? If yes, a moat likely exists.

Moats and the DSE In the Daily Wrap, we note when a company’s loan growth outpaces peers (branch network moat) or when TBL’s volumes hold up despite a price increase (brand loyalty). Moats are not permanent — technology, regulation, or poor management can erode them.

2.6 How Management Decisions Create (or Destroy) Value

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Even a great company with a wide moat can be ruined by poor management. Conversely, a mediocre business can be transformed by an exceptional leadership team.

Five Key Management Decisions

Capital allocation: Invests in high-return projects; returns excess cash to shareholders. Warning: chasing growth for its own sake, expensive acquisitions.

Capital structure: Maintains prudent debt; matches funding to asset life. Warning: excessive short-term debt.

Dividend policy: Sustainable, growing dividends, retaining enough for reinvestment. Warning: sudden cuts without explanation; borrowing to pay dividends.

Transparency: Open about strategy, risks, performance. Warning: complex language, frequent accounting changes.

Culture & Governance: Ethical, long-oriented; independent board. Warning: conflicts of interest, family members in key roles without merit.

Assessing Management on the DSE

Read the chairman’s and CEO’s letters. Are they honest about challenges?

Compare ROE over time. Consistent ROE suggests disciplined capital allocation.

Check insider trading announcements. Heavy director selling without reason is a red flag.

Review dividend history. Stable or growing dividends signal confidence.

3.1 What Is a Stock Exchange? Inside the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

We now shift to the arena where companies and investors meet. The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange (DSE) is the marketplace where ownership of Tanzanian companies is bought and sold.

Purpose of a Stock Exchange

Capital raising: Companies sell shares to the public (IPO, rights issue) for expansion.

Liquidity: Investors can buy and sell shares easily.

Price discovery: Through thousands of buyers and sellers, the market determines fair prices.

The DSE operates under CMSA regulation.

The DSE: Brief History and Structure Incorporated in 1996, trading began in 1998. It now lists over 20 companies, including cross-listings from Kenya (KCB, NMG). Trading days are Monday to Friday, 10:00 am to 3:00 pm EAT. The automated trading system matches orders; settlement is through the Central Securities Depository (CSD), which holds shares electronically.

DSE Indices

DSE All Share Index (DSEI): Tracks all listed companies.

Tanzania Share Index (TSI): Excludes cross-listed companies.

Sector indices: BI (banks), IA (industrials), CS (commercial services).

Every Daily DSE Wrap reports these indices so you can see which segments are moving.

Retail investors

Institutional investors (pension funds, insurance, UTT AMIS)

Brokers and market makers (developing)

3.2 Stocks, Bonds, and ETFs: The Three Building Blocks

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

On the DSE, you buy specific securities, each with its own risk, return, and purpose.

Stocks (Equities) Buying a share of CRDB or TBL makes you a part-owner. You share in profits (dividends, capital gains) and risks. Stocks offer the highest long-term return potential but are the most volatile.

Bonds A bond is a loan to the government or a company in exchange for fixed interest (coupon) and return of principal at maturity. Bonds are generally safer, offering lower returns. Portfolio Building A balanced investor might hold 60% equities, 30% bonds, 10% cash. The exact mix depends on age, goals, and risk tolerance.

3.3 How a Trade Happens: From Your Phone to the Market

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You decide to buy 100 shares of CRDB. What happens next?

Placing the Order Contact a licensed DSE stockbroker and specify:

Ticker (e.g., CRDB)

Quantity (number of shares)

Price limit (maximum you will pay, or minimum you will accept if selling) Your broker enters the order into the DSE’s Automated Trading System (ATS).

The Order Book The ATS maintains an electronic order book — lists of all outstanding buy (bid) and sell (ask/offer) orders, sorted by price and time. The best bid is the highest price any buyer will pay; the best ask is the lowest any seller will accept. The difference is the spread. A trade occurs when a buy order matches a sell order.

In the DSE daily price tables, you see “Outstanding Bids” and “Outstanding Offers” — the depth of demand and supply at the close. For a stock like CRDB, a deep bid side (e.g., 225,734 bids on 29 June versus 82,285 offers) tells you buyers are more eager than sellers, which can push the price up. For a less liquid stock, a thin bid side means selling even a modest number of shares could push the price down significantly.

Clearing and Settlement Once executed, the DSE sends details to the Central Securities Depository (CSD). Ownership records update: shares move from seller’s account to yours, cash moves between brokers’ settlement accounts. This takes T+2 (trade date plus two business days). Your shares are held electronically; you receive a contract note.

Why This Matters Understanding mechanics helps you set realistic limit orders. If the spread is wide (e.g., bid at 2,550, ask at 2,650), a limit order at the midpoint may not fill quickly. High volume and many deals signal strong liquidity; low volume with wide spreads suggests caution.

3.5 The Difference Between Primary and Secondary Markets

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

When you buy CRDB shares through your broker on a Tuesday, you’re in the secondary market — trading already-issued securities. But CRDB originally sold shares to the public in an IPO in the primary market.

Primary Market: Companies Raise Fresh Capital The issuer sells new securities directly to investors. Proceeds go to the company. Examples:

IPO (Initial Public Offering): A company lists on the DSE for the first time.

Rights issue: Existing shareholders buy additional shares at a discount.

Bond auction: Government or corporation issues new bonds.

Primary offerings affect existing shareholders (dilution in a rights issue) and signal growth plans. The Daily Wrap covers these when announced.

Secondary Market: Investors Trade Among Themselves This is the DSE’s daily trading — buying and selling between investors. No new capital flows to the company. It provides liquidity and fair price discovery. The DSEI reflects secondary market prices.

Why the Distinction Matters

IPO shares are often priced at a discount; secondary trading determines the real price.

A rights issue dilutes your ownership unless you participate.

Buying a bond at auction and holding to maturity avoids price fluctuations; buying in the secondary market exposes you to interest rate risk.

4.1 What Is Fundamental Analysis? The Art of Finding True Value

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You now understand the economy, companies, and the marketplace. Fundamental analysis is the process of evaluating a company’s intrinsic value by examining its financial statements, competitive position, industry, and the broader economy. If intrinsic value is significantly above the market price, the stock may be undervalued — a buying opportunity.

Price vs. Value Benjamin Graham taught that in the short run the market is a voting machine (popularity), but in the long run it is a weighing machine (substance). Your job is to estimate substance — the true worth — and compare it to the current market price. Intrinsic value is a range, based on reasonable assumptions about future cash flows, growth, and risk.

Two Main Approaches

Relative valuation: Compare the company’s price to earnings, book value, or sales, then compare those multiples to peers or history (covered in 4.2).

Discounted cash flow (DCF): Project future free cash flows and discount them back to today’s shillings. More complex but forces deep thinking.

For most DSE investors, relative valuation using simple multiples is a practical start.

4.2 Key Valuation Ratios Made Simple (P/E, P/B, Dividend Yield)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Ratios are the shorthand of fundamental analysis. They let you compare companies of different sizes and assess whether a stock is cheap or expensive at a glance.

Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E) P/E = Share Price / Earnings per Share (EPS). It tells you how many years of current earnings it would take to recoup the purchase price. High P/E suggests expected strong future growth; low P/E may signal a bargain — or a company in trouble. On the DSE, banks often trade at P/E ratios between 5 and 12. If CRDB earns TZS 250 per share and trades at TZS 2,590, its P/E is about 10.4 — reasonable for a stable bank in a growing economy.

Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B) P/B = Share Price / Book Value per Share. Book value is assets minus liabilities — net worth per share. P/B below 1.0 means the market values the company at less than its accounting net worth. Banks are often analysed using P/B. If NMB trades at TZS 16,160 and has a book value per share of, say, TZS 10,000, its P/B is 1.62. That premium suggests the market expects NMB to generate returns well above its book value.

Dividend Yield Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Share Price × 100%. This tells you the cash return just for holding the stock. On 29 June, TBL traded at TZS 9,710 and paid an annual dividend of TZS 818, a yield of 8.4%. High yield can be a trap if the dividend is unsustainable.

Using Ratios Wisely Compare a company’s ratios to its own history, industry peers, and the overall market. The Daily Wrap occasionally references valuation context — like “the banking sector trades at a discount to regional peers on a price-to-book basis.”

4.6 An Introduction to Technical Analysis (It’s Not Sorcery)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

While fundamental analysis tells you what to buy, technical analysis attempts to tell you when by studying price charts, volume, and patterns. It’s a supplementary tool, not a crystal ball.

The Foundation: Price and Trends A trend is the general direction of prices. An uptrend has higher highs and higher lows; a downtrend has lower lows and lower highs. The simplest rule: Don’t fight the trend. A rising price with rising volume suggests accumulation; a falling price with high volume suggests distribution.

Support and Resistance

Support: A price level where buying interest prevents further decline (a floor).

Resistance: A price level where selling pressure halts a rally (a ceiling).

If CRDB consistently finds buyers at TZS 2,550, that’s support; if it fails to break above TZS 2,600, that’s resistance.

Volume as Confirmation A breakout above resistance on significantly higher volume is more likely genuine. A breakout on low volume may be false.

Simple Moving Averages (SMA) A 50-day SMA is the average closing price over the last 50 trading days, smoothing noise. Price above the 50-day SMA suggests a positive medium-term trend; below, negative.

Limitations on the DSE Technical analysis works best in liquid, continuous markets. The DSE’s lower liquidity on some counters means a few large orders can create misleading patterns. Use it as a supplement to, never a replacement for, fundamental analysis.

5.3 The Power of Compounding and Why Time Beats Timing

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Einstein reportedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. For a Tanzanian investor, compounding is the single most powerful wealth-building force.

What Is Compounding? Earning returns on your returns. Invest TZS 1,000,000 at 10%: after one year, TZS 1,100,000. Next year, you earn 10% on TZS 1,100,000 (TZS 110,000), not on the original TZS 1,000,000. Over years, this snowballs.

The Mathematics TZS 1,000,000 at 10% annual return: 5 years → TZS 1,610,510; 10 years → TZS 2,593,742; 20 years → TZS 6,727,500; 30 years → TZS 17,449,402. Notice the last 10 years added over TZS 10 million with no new money. Reinvesting dividends accelerates this further.

Time in the Market vs. Timing the Market Trying to buy at the bottom and sell at the top rarely works consistently. Missing just the best 10 trading days in a decade can halve your returns. The fully invested, dividend-reinvesting, noise-ignoring investor almost always outperforms the trader.

5.4 Behavioural Finance: Understanding Your Own Worst Enemy

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You can master all analysis techniques and still fail if you cannot master your own emotions. Behavioural finance studies psychological biases that cause irrational decisions.

Loss Aversion Pain of losing is about twice as powerful as pleasure of gaining. This leads to holding onto losers too long and selling winners too early. Fix: Have a predetermined thesis for every investment. If the thesis is broken, sell regardless. If intact, hold through noise.

Herd Mentality The temptation to buy when everyone is buying and sell when everyone is panicking. Herding creates bubbles and crashes. Fix: Use a checklist (Article 4.7) and a long-term plan. Re-read your written investment policy.

Overconfidence After a few successes, investors believe they have a special talent for prediction. They trade more, take larger positions, and suffer disproportionate losses. Fix: Track your decisions. Compare your actual returns to the DSEI. If not beating the index over a cycle, simplify and diversify.

Confirmation Bias Noticing only information that supports your existing view, ignoring contradictory evidence. Fix: Actively seek the bear case for every stock you own.

6.3 Understanding Tanzanian Tax on Investments

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Taxes affect your net return. Tanzania’s tax regime for listed securities is relatively favourable.

Withholding Tax on Dividends Dividends from DSE-listed companies are subject to a 5% final withholding tax for resident individuals. If a company declares TZS 1,000 per share, TZS 950 is deposited, TZS 50 goes to TRA. The tax is final — you do not declare it further. For non-residents, rates vary under double taxation agreements (5–15%). For example, a UK-based investor might pay 15% withholding on dividends, so a TZS 1,000 gross dividend yields TZS 850 net.

Capital Gains Tax on Listed Securities Currently, capital gains from the sale of listed securities are exempt from tax for both residents and non-residents. This is a major advantage. A gain of TZS 1,000,000 is yours entirely. This exemption encourages long-term investment and market participation. Tax laws can change, so stay informed.

Withholding Tax on Bond Interest Interest on government and corporate bonds is subject to a 5% withholding tax for residents. For non-residents, the rate may be higher under certain conditions. For example, a 15.95% government bond coupon would yield approximately 15.15% net for a resident after the 5% withholding tax.

Tax on Collective Investment Schemes If you invest through a unit trust like UTT AMIS, the tax treatment depends on the underlying assets. Generally, fund income distributed to you is subject to withholding tax at the applicable rate.

Record-Keeping Keep all contract notes, dividend vouchers, and annual CSD statements. They are your proof of transactions and will be needed if audited or if you need to reconcile your portfolio.

How a Unit Trust Works

You buy units at the prevailing unit price (Net Asset Value per unit, NAV).

The fund invests in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or both.

Advantages

Instant diversification even with small amounts.

Equity funds: Invest primarily in stocks. Higher risk, higher potential return.

6.5 Building a Monthly Investment Habit

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

The most successful investors are the most consistent. Building a monthly investment habit — automatically, regardless of market conditions — is the surest path to long-term wealth.

Shilling-Cost Averaging Instead of investing a large lump sum at once, you invest a fixed amount at regular intervals (e.g., TZS 200,000 every month). When prices are high, your fixed amount buys fewer shares; when prices are low, it buys more. Over time, your average cost per share is lower than the average price, because you automatically bought more during dips. This removes the need to time the market and reduces emotional stress.

How to Set It Up

Determine your monthly investment amount (what you can comfortably set aside after essential expenses).

Choose one or two well-diversified instruments: a balanced unit trust, or 2–3 blue-chip stocks you intend to hold for years.

Set up a standing order from your bank account to your brokerage or fund account on a fixed date each month.

Instruct your broker to buy the specified shares or fund units as soon as the funds arrive.

Even TZS 100,000 per month will compound into substantial wealth over decades.

The Psychological Benefit Monthly investing removes constant decision-making. You are no longer asking, “Is now a good time to buy?” because the answer is always yes — you’re investing for 2035, not next week. 7.2 Navigating DSE Liquidity: Small-Cap and Illiquid Stocks

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Liquidity is the ability to buy or sell an asset quickly without significantly moving the price. On the DSE, liquidity varies dramatically. CRDB and NMB often trade hundreds of thousands of shares daily; some counters trade only a few hundred in a week. Ignoring liquidity is one of the fastest ways to lose money.

Understanding DSE Liquidity Tiers

High (blue-chip): CRDB, NMB, TBL, VODA, KCB. You can usually trade a few hundred shares near the quoted price. Spreads are narrow.

Moderate: TCC, TPCC, TOL, SWIS, NICO. You may need patience; orders of more than 50–100 shares could move the price.

Low (small-cap): DCB, MUCOBA, MBP, PAL, TTP. A single order of a few thousand shillings can change the price by several percent. Spreads are wide.

The Cost of Illiquidity: The Bid-Ask Spread The spread is the gap between what buyers will pay (bid) and what sellers ask (offer). On liquid stocks, it might be 0.1–0.5%; on illiquid stocks, 5–10% or more. If you buy at the offer and immediately sell at the bid, you lose the spread. You must overcome this built-in loss before making a profit.

Practical Rules for Illiquid Counters

Use limit orders only. Never place a market order on a low-volume stock.

Trade smaller sizes. If daily volume is 2,000 shares, don’t try to buy 5,000 in one go.

Be patient. Your order may remain unfilled for days or weeks. That’s normal.

Check outstanding bids and offers. If there are zero bids, do not buy — you won’t be able to sell later without taking a loss.

Liquidity and Valuation Illiquid stocks often appear cheap on P/E or P/B, but that “discount” is the market charging you for illiquidity. Don’t mistake a low price for a bargain. Always ask: If I needed to sell tomorrow, could I do so without losing a large chunk of my capital?

7.4 Corporate Actions Beyond Dividends: Rights Issues, Splits, and Bonus Shares

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Dividends are the most common corporate action, but companies also use other mechanisms that affect your holdings. Understanding them is critical.

Rights Issue A company offers existing shareholders the right to buy additional shares at a discount to the market price, in proportion to their current holding (e.g., 1 new share for every 5 held). This raises fresh capital for the company. If you participate, you maintain your ownership percentage and buy at a discount. If you do not, your ownership is diluted (your percentage of the company shrinks), and the value of your existing shares may fall because more shares now exist. Rights are usually renounceable — you can sell your rights in the market if you choose not to take up the offer.

Bonus Issue (Scrip Issue) Also called a capitalisation issue, a bonus issue gives existing shareholders additional shares for free, in proportion to their holding (e.g., 1 bonus share for every 10 held). No new capital is raised; the company simply converts reserves (retained earnings) into share capital. Your ownership percentage does not change, and the share price typically adjusts downward to reflect the increased number of shares, leaving the total value of your holding unchanged. However, bonus issues can improve liquidity and signal management confidence.

Share Split A company divides its existing shares into multiple shares (e.g., a 2-for-1 split doubles the number of shares and halves the price). The total value of your holding stays the same. Splits are often done to make shares more affordable and improve liquidity, which can attract more investors.

Mergers and Acquisitions When two companies combine, shareholders of the acquired company may receive cash, shares in the acquiring company, or a mix. These events can create or destroy value depending on the terms. Always evaluate whether the offer fairly values your shares.

What to Do When a Corporate Action Is Announced

Read the circular from the company carefully.

Understand the impact on your ownership and the share price.

For rights issues, decide whether to participate, sell your rights, or do nothing (the last option usually leads to dilution).

Check the record date — you must hold shares before this date to be entitled.

If uncertain, consult your broker or a financial advisor.

7.5 Green Bonds and ESG Investing on the DSE

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Global capital is shifting toward sustainable investing, and Tanzania is part of this movement. ESG stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance — factors that measure a company’s sustainability and ethical impact. On the DSE, this trend is visible through green bonds and growing governance expectations.

What Are Green Bonds? Green bonds are debt instruments where the proceeds are exclusively used for environmentally beneficial projects — solar farms, reforestation, clean water, sustainable agriculture. The Tanzanian government and some corporations have begun issuing green and sustainable bonds. The DSE lists outstanding sustainable bonds with a face value of over TZS 531 billion (as of June 2026). The Government of Tanzania has also issued infrastructure bonds that may have green components.

Why ESG Matters for Investors

Risk management: Companies with poor governance (corruption, opaque accounting) or environmental liabilities (pollution) face fines, reputational damage, and higher costs.

Return potential: Sustainable companies may be better positioned for long-term growth as regulations tighten and consumer preferences shift.

Access to capital: International investors increasingly demand ESG compliance. DSE companies that adopt strong ESG practices may attract more foreign capital, potentially boosting their share prices.

ESG on the DSE While formal ESG ratings are not yet widespread in Tanzania, you can assess companies yourself:

Environmental: Does the company have a policy on emissions, waste, water usage? Does it face environmental litigation?

Social: How does it treat employees, communities, and customers? Does it promote financial inclusion?

Governance: Is the board independent? Are there conflicts of interest? Is executive pay reasonable and transparent?

Look for disclosures in annual reports and sustainability reports. Companies with strong governance — such as those with diverse, independent boards and clear audit trails — are generally lower risk.

Green Bond Investing Green bonds trade on the DSE like conventional bonds. They offer fixed coupons and may provide an additional feel-good factor, but you should still assess them based on yield, credit risk, and liquidity. The sustainable bond market in Tanzania is growing, and being aware of it positions you at the forefront of a global trend.
