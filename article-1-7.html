Article 1.7 - How Global Markets Affect the DSE

By Amana Capital East Africa - Investor Education Series

The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange does not operate in a bubble. Every day, events in Washington, London, Beijing, and Nairobi ripple through the Tanzanian market, affecting the prices of stocks and bonds you hold. This article explains the main channels through which global markets reach the DSE, so you can understand the “why” behind the daily numbers.

1. The US Federal Reserve and global interest rates When the US central bank (the Federal Reserve) raises or lowers its benchmark interest rate, money flows around the world in response. A rise in US rates makes American bonds more attractive, drawing capital away from frontier markets like Tanzania. This can lead to foreign selling on the DSE, particularly in large, liquid stocks like CRDB and NMB. Conversely, when US rates fall, international investors hunt for higher yields, and Tanzanian equities and bonds can benefit.

In our Daily DSE Wrap, the foreign flow table tells you whether international money is entering or leaving. When you see a sustained period of foreign selling, the driver is often a change in global interest rate expectations.

2. Commodity prices Tanzania exports gold, cashews, coffee, and other commodities. It imports oil, machinery, and manufactured goods. A rise in global oil prices makes transport and manufacturing more expensive for DSE-listed companies, squeezing margins. A rise in gold prices brings foreign currency into the country, strengthening the shilling and benefiting the broader economy.

3. Exchange rates As discussed in our earlier article on exchange rates, the TZS/USD rate directly affects the profitability of importers (like TOL) and the purchasing power of diaspora investors. Global events - such as a Bank of England rate decision or a political crisis in Europe - can move the TZS/GBP and TZS/EUR rates, which in turn affect your portfolio.

4. Cross-border investment and regional integration The DSE lists several cross-listed stocks from Kenya, including KCB and Nation Media Group. Developments in Nairobi - whether a change in Kenyan interest rates, a new regulation, or a political event - can influence the price of these stocks on the DSE. As East African integration deepens, the link between regional markets will only strengthen.

5. Global risk appetite International investors categorise Tanzania as a “frontier market”. When global sentiment is positive and investors are willing to take risks, frontier markets tend to attract capital. When fear grips global markets - during a pandemic, a war, or a financial crisis - frontier markets are often the first to see capital flee. Understanding this rhythm helps you avoid panic. Selling during a global risk-off event often means selling at the worst possible moment. Patient, long-horizon investors who hold through these cycles are typically rewarded.

6. Practical application When you read the Daily DSE Wrap, note the foreign flow percentages and the exchange rates. Ask yourself: What global event might be driving these numbers? Over time, you will build a mental map that connects global headlines to the DSE’s daily movements.
