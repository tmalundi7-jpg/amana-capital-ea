The Basics: What Drives Price and Quantity?

Supply is how much of a product producers are willing to sell at a given price. Higher prices incentivise more supply.

Demand is how much consumers are willing to buy at a given price. Lower prices attract more demand.

Equilibrium is where the two curves meet. That�s the market price and quantity.

For a business, the goal is to sell at a price well above the cost of production. The difference is profit.

Pricing Power: The Investor�s Best Friend

Not all companies can raise prices without losing customers. A company with pricing power can increase prices steadily over time without significantly reducing demand. This power comes from:

Strong brand: Consumers pay more for a trusted brand. TBL�s Safari Lager commands loyalty that a generic beer cannot.

Monopoly or oligopoly position: When few competitors exist, pricing power is high. Tanzania�s cement industry has limited players, giving firms like TCC and TPCC some pricing ability.

High switching costs: If a customer cannot easily move to a competitor, the company has power. A bank where a client holds a mortgage, salary account, and mobile banking profile is hard to leave.

Companies with pricing power are generally better investments because they can protect margins during inflationary periods and reinvest profits for growth.

How Demand Shifts and What It Means

Demand can shift for many reasons: rising incomes, changing tastes, population growth, or new technology. For example:

Rising incomes: As Tanzania�s middle class expands, demand for financial services, mobile data, and branded consumer goods increases. This benefits banks like CRDB and NMB, and telcos like VODA.

Demographic shifts: A young, urbanising population drives demand for housing (cement), entertainment, and mobile money.

Regulatory changes: A new tax on mobile money transactions could reduce demand for that service, hitting telco revenues.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, when we note that a particular sector is rallying, demand-side factors are often the underlying driver. We track turnover and volume because they reveal where demand is flowing in real time.

Supply-Side Factors: What Can Go Wrong

Even great demand won�t help a company that can�t deliver. Supply disruptions � drought for agricultural inputs, power outages for manufacturers, import delays for raw materials � can crush margins. Companies that manage their supply chains well, diversify their suppliers, and maintain inventory buffers are more resilient.

When analysing a DSE company, ask:

Where do its raw materials come from?

How dependent is it on imports?

Does it have a reliable and affordable supply of electricity and water?
