Article 2.2 - How to Read a Company’s Financial Statements (Part 1 - The Income Statement)

By Amana Capital East Africa - Investor Education Series

Financial statements are the report card of a business. The first and most watched is the income statement (profit and loss statement). It answers: Is this company making money?

Anatomy of an Income Statement Every income statement follows this structure:

Revenue (Sales): Total money from selling goods or services.

- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): Direct costs of production.

= Gross Profit

- Operating Expenses: Salaries, rent, marketing, depreciation.

= Operating Profit (EBIT)

- Interest Expense + Interest Income - Tax

= Net Profit

For a Tanzanian bank like CRDB, interest income from loans is revenue; interest paid on deposits is COGS; the difference is net interest income. After branch and staff costs, we get operating profit.

Margins: The True Test

Gross Margin = (Gross Profit / Revenue) × 100.

Operating Margin = (Operating Profit / Revenue) × 100.

Net Margin = (Net Profit / Revenue) × 100.

Compare margins over time and across competitors. A declining margin may signal rising costs, competition, or poor management. A stable or expanding margin is positive.

Real DSE Application When you read an annual report, ask: Is revenue growing? Are costs growing faster than revenue? Is net profit sustainable or boosted by one-off items? The Daily DSE Wrap often notes earnings results - for example, if NMB’s net interest margin improved due to lower funding costs, that’s the income statement at work.
