Article 2.3 - How to Read a Company’s Financial Statements (Part 2 - The Balance Sheet)

By Amana Capital East Africa - Investor Education Series

The balance sheet shows what a company owns and owes at a single point in time. It reveals hidden strengths or looming dangers the income statement alone misses.

The Fundamental Equation Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders’ Equity

Assets are what the company owns: cash, buildings, inventory, receivables.

Liabilities are what it owes: loans, supplier payables, bonds.

Shareholders’ Equity is the net worth left for owners after debts are paid.

Key Items

Cash and equivalents: Can the company survive a shock?

Accounts receivable: Money owed by customers. Fast-growing receivables may signal collection problems.

Inventory: Too much ties up cash; too little may lose sales.

Property, plant & equipment (PPE): Fixed assets. Heavy investment signals expansion but also depreciation.

Short-term debt: Must be manageable relative to cash and cash flow.

Long-term debt: Excessive debt can choke a company in a downturn.

Retained earnings: Cumulative profits not paid as dividends; show how much has been reinvested.

The Debt-to-Equity Ratio Total Liabilities / Shareholders’ Equity. A ratio above 2.0 is high for non-financial companies. Banks naturally have higher leverage. Check this ratio against peers and over time. For example, TCC and TPCC, as cement manufacturers, may carry significant debt for plant construction, but their debt should be serviceable from operating cash flow.

Working Capital Current Assets - Current Liabilities. Positive working capital means the company can meet short-term obligations. A worsening trend may signal collection or inventory problems. Watch for a sudden spike in receivables without a corresponding rise in revenue - that could mean customers are delaying payments, tying up cash.

Daily DSE Wrap Connection When we say “CRDB has a strong capital base,” that’s a balance sheet statement. The health of the balance sheet determines whether a company can pay dividends, invest, or survive a recession.
