he Cash Flow Statement: Where the Money Really Is

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. A company can report a healthy net profit on its income statement but still go bankrupt if it runs out of cash. The cash flow statement shows the actual movement of money in and out of the business. For a DSE investor, it’s the ultimate truth‑test.

The Three Sections of the Cash Flow Statement

Every cash flow statement is divided into three parts:

Operating Activities: Cash generated from the core business — selling goods or services, paying salaries, collecting from customers. This is the most important section. Positive operating cash flow is the sign of a healthy, self‑sustaining business.

Investing Activities: Cash spent on buying assets (new factories, equipment) or received from selling them. Negative investing cash flow is not necessarily bad — it may mean the company is expanding. But it must be funded by operating cash flow or prudent borrowing.

Financing Activities: Cash raised from issuing shares or taking loans, and cash paid out as dividends or debt repayments. This section shows how the company funds its operations and growth.

Free Cash Flow: The Investor’s Key Metric

Free Cash Flow (FCF) = Operating Cash Flow – Capital Expenditures (CapEx).

FCF is the cash a company has left after maintaining or expanding its asset base. This cash can be used to pay dividends, buy back shares, reduce debt, or reinvest for further growth. Companies that consistently generate strong free cash flow are highly prized by investors.

When you read about a company’s results, look for FCF. A company that reports rising profits but declining or negative FCF may be using aggressive accounting to inflate earnings. Cash flow does not lie.

Real DSE Application

Consider a cement company. It might report strong operating cash flow because it sells cement at a profit. But if it is building a new plant, its investing cash flow will be heavily negative — it’s spending cash on expansion. That’s acceptable as long as the expansion is likely to generate future returns. However, if the company is borrowing heavily to fund that expansion and operating cash flow is weak, it’s a red flag.

In our Daily DSE Wrap, when we mention a company’s capital expenditure plans or dividend declaration, we are indirectly referencing the cash flow statement. The ability to pay a growing dividend almost always requires strong free cash flow.

The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In the Daily Wrap, we regularly report dividend announcements and corporate actions. Those are direct outcomes of cash flow decisions. When TBL declares a TZS 818 per share dividend, the company is signalling that it generated enough cash to reward shareholders. When NMB retains earnings to fund regional expansion, that’s a financing decision visible in the cash flow statement.

Action step: For any DSE stock you hold, look up its latest cash flow statement. Calculate free cash flow. Is it positive? Is it growing? Compare it to net profit. If FCF is far below net profit, investigate why.
