Article 3.3 - How a Trade Happens: From Your Phone to the Market

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You decide to buy 100 shares of CRDB. What happens next?

Placing the Order Contact a licensed DSE stockbroker and specify:

  • Ticker (e.g., CRDB)
  • Quantity (number of shares)
  • Price limit (maximum you will pay, or minimum you will accept if selling) Your broker enters the order into the DSE’s Automated Trading System (ATS).

The Order Book The ATS maintains an electronic order book — lists of all outstanding buy (bid) and sell (ask/offer) orders, sorted by price and time. The best bid is the highest price any buyer will pay; the best ask is the lowest any seller will accept. The difference is the spread. A trade occurs when a buy order matches a sell order.

In the DSE daily price tables, you see “Outstanding Bids” and “Outstanding Offers” — the depth of demand and supply at the close. For a stock like CRDB, a deep bid side (e.g., 225,734 bids on 29 June versus 82,285 offers) tells you buyers are more eager than sellers, which can push the price up. For a less liquid stock, a thin bid side means selling even a modest number of shares could push the price down significantly.

Clearing and Settlement Once executed, the DSE sends details to the Central Securities Depository (CSD). Ownership records update: shares move from seller’s account to yours, cash moves between brokers’ settlement accounts. This takes T+2 (trade date plus two business days). Your shares are held electronically; you receive a contract note.

Why This Matters Understanding mechanics helps you set realistic limit orders. If the spread is wide (e.g., bid at 2,550, ask at 2,650), a limit order at the midpoint may not fill quickly. High volume and many deals signal strong liquidity; low volume with wide spreads suggests caution.

Action Step: Look at the DSE daily price table on our website. Pick a stock and note the spread. Is it narrow (liquid) or wide (illiquid)?
