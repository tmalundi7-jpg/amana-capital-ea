nderstanding Market Data: Turnover, Volume, and Deals

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Every trading day, the DSE publishes numbers that look like this: “Turnover: TZS 7,088.98 million, Volume: 1,734,477 shares, Deals: 4,704.” These figures appear in our Daily DSE Wrap and in our Current Prices table. They are not just statistics — they are the market’s voice. This article teaches you to listen.

Turnover: The Money Flow

Turnover is the total value of shares traded in a session. It’s the most important liquidity indicator. A rising turnover trend over several weeks suggests growing investor interest and confidence. A sudden spike in turnover without a corresponding price move may indicate a large institutional trade (block trade) or an accumulation/distribution phase.

In the 29 June 2026 market, equity turnover was TZS 7.09 billion. This was down sharply from the previous session’s record, but still well above the TZS 3 billion that marks a quiet day. The high turnover tells us the market is liquid and that meaningful money is flowing.

Volume: The Quantity of Shares Traded

Volume is the total number of shares that changed hands. Compare volume to the number of outstanding shares to gauge how active a stock is. A high volume on a stock like CRDB (928,067 shares on 29 June) indicates deep liquidity — you can buy or sell a large block without moving the price dramatically. A low volume on a smaller counter means even a modest order could push the price.

Volume can confirm a trend. A rising price accompanied by expanding volume is a strong signal of genuine demand. A rising price on declining volume may be fragile.

Deals: The Participation Count

The number of deals tells you how many separate buy‑sell transactions occurred. A high deal count relative to volume suggests broad retail participation — many investors making small trades. A low deal count with very high volume suggests large block trades between institutions.

On 29 June, 4,704 deals were recorded despite volume being moderate. That indicates a lot of small retail and local institutional orders — precisely the kind of broad‑based participation that signals a healthy, domestically driven market.

Putting It All Together

Consider this scenario: Turnover surges, volume jumps, deals spike, and the DSEI rises. Interpretation: strong, broad‑based buying with institutional and retail participation. The rally is likely sustainable.

Now imagine: Turnover rises, but volume and deals fall, and the DSEI barely moves. That might be a single large cross‑trade (block) that doesn’t reflect genuine market direction.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we combine these three metrics — turnover, volume, deals — with the index movements to tell the day’s story. When you read, “Deal count surged to 4,704, signalling broad retail and local institutional participation,” you now know exactly what that means.

Action step: In today’s Daily Wrap, note the three numbers. Are they moving in the same direction? What story do they tell?
