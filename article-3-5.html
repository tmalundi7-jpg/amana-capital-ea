he Difference Between Primary and Secondary Markets

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

When you buy shares of CRDB through your broker on a Tuesday afternoon, you are participating in the secondary market — the day‑to‑day exchange of already‑issued securities. But CRDB originally sold its shares to the public in an IPO (initial public offering) in the primary market. Understanding the distinction is essential because each market serves a different purpose and offers different opportunities.

The Primary Market: Companies Raise Fresh Capital

In the primary market, a company or government issues new securities and sells them directly to investors. The proceeds go to the issuer. This happens through:

IPO (Initial Public Offering): A company lists on the DSE for the first time.

Rights issue: Existing shareholders are given the right to buy additional shares at a discount, in proportion to their current holding. This is a way to raise capital from those already invested.

Bond auction: The government or a corporation issues new bonds, and investors bid for them.

Primary market offerings are often covered in the Daily DSE Wrap when they are announced, because they can affect existing shareholders (dilution in a rights issue) and signal management’s growth plans.

The Secondary Market: Investors Trade Among Themselves

The secondary market is the DSE’s daily trading activity — all the buying and selling you see in the wrap. Money changes hands between investors; the company does not receive any new capital. The secondary market provides liquidity, allowing you to exit an investment or build a position at a fair, transparent price.

The DSE All Share Index (DSEI) reflects secondary market prices. When the index rises, the value of existing holdings goes up, even though no new capital flowed to the companies.

Why the Distinction Matters

Valuation context: Shares bought in an IPO are often priced at a discount to encourage participation. After listing, secondary market trading determines the real price. A share that “pops” on listing may have been undervalued in the IPO.

Dilution awareness: A rights issue in the primary market increases the number of shares outstanding. If you don’t participate, your ownership percentage shrinks. You must decide whether to invest more to maintain your stake.

Bond strategies: Buying a government bond at auction (primary) and holding to maturity avoids secondary market price fluctuations. Buying in the secondary market allows you to sell before maturity but exposes you to interest rate risk.

The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In the Daily Wrap, we report the secondary market data every day. When primary market events occur — an announced IPO, a rights issue, a bond auction — we provide dedicated analysis. Understanding the distinction helps you interpret that analysis correctly.

Action step: Think about your own portfolio. Did you acquire your shares in the secondary market? If your company announces a rights issue, will you participate, or will you accept dilution? Knowing the difference now prepares you for that decision.
