Article 3.6 - How to Invest in Government and Corporate Bonds on the DSE

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Bonds are the quiet foundation of many successful portfolios. They provide a steady stream of income, can protect your capital when stock markets fall, and in Tanzania they come with a major tax advantage. Yet many investors overlook them simply because they seem more complicated than buying shares. This article explains exactly what bonds are, how they work on the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange, and how you can invest in them.

What is a bond?

A bond is a loan. When you buy a government bond, you are lending money to the Tanzanian government. In return, the government promises to pay you a fixed interest payment (called a coupon) every year until the bond matures, at which point you get your original capital back. Corporate bonds work the same way, except the borrower is a company rather than the government.

Key terms you need to know

Face value: The amount you will receive when the bond matures. Usually TZS100,000 or TZS1,000,000 per bond.

Coupon: The annual interest payment, expressed as a percentage of the face value. For example, a 15.95% coupon on a 25-year bond means you receive TZS15,950 each year for every TZS100,000 of face value you hold.

Price: Bonds trade on the DSE at prices that can be above or below face value, depending on how interest rates have moved since the bond was issued. If you buy a bond at 90% of face value and hold to maturity, you earn an extra gain on top of the coupon.

Yield to maturity: The total return you will earn each year if you hold the bond until it matures, taking into account both the coupon and any gain or loss from the price you paid.

Why Tanzanian government bonds are especially attractive

Interest earned on government bonds with a maturity of three years or longer is completely exempt from withholding tax. That means a 15.95% coupon delivers 15.95% into your pocket — no deductions. For a resident investor in the highest tax bracket, that is a powerful advantage over bank deposits or dividend income, which are taxed at 5%.

How to buy bonds on the DSE

You can buy bonds in two ways: at the primary auction, or on the secondary market.

Primary auction: The Bank of Tanzania holds regular bond auctions on behalf of the government. You submit a bid through a licensed stockbroker, stating how much you want to invest and the yield you are willing to accept. If your bid is successful, you receive the bonds at the auction price. This is ideal for investors who want to buy and hold to maturity.

Secondary market: After bonds are issued, they trade on the DSE just like shares. You can buy or sell bonds at any time through your broker, at the prevailing market price. This gives you flexibility, but also means the price can fluctuate before maturity. If you plan to sell before maturity, you need to watch interest rate movements: when rates rise, bond prices fall, and vice versa.

How to place a bond order

The process is the same as placing a stock order. You contact your broker, specify the bond (by its series or coupon and maturity date), the quantity, and the price you are willing to pay. For government bonds, the minimum investment can be as low as TZS100,000 face value, though brokers may have their own minimums.

Corporate bonds on the DSE

Several Tanzanian companies have issued bonds that trade on the exchange. These typically offer higher coupons than government bonds, but they carry credit risk — the possibility that the company might fail to make its payments. Always check the company’s credit rating and financial health before investing.

Using a bond fund for simplicity

If you prefer not to pick individual bonds, you can invest in a bond unit trust like the UTT AMIS Bond Fund. The fund pools money from many investors and buys a diversified portfolio of government and corporate bonds. You earn a share of the interest, and the fund handles all the buying, selling, and record-keeping. This is an excellent starting point for beginners.

Bonds and your overall portfolio

Bonds are not just for conservative investors. Even if you are building long-term wealth through equities, holding a portion of your portfolio in bonds reduces the overall ups and downs and gives you a source of cash to reinvest when stock prices fall. The daily DSE Wrap reports bond turnover and yields so you can monitor the market alongside equities.

Put It Into Practice

Contact your broker and ask about the next government bond auction. Find out the minimum investment, the auction date, and how to submit a bid. Even if you decide not to invest this time, going through the process once will teach you more than any article can.
