Article 3.8 - How to Read the Official DSE Daily Report

By Amana Capital East Africa - Investor Education Series

Every trading day, the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange publishes a market report that contains a wealth of information. On the surface, it can look like a wall of numbers. But once you understand its structure, you can extract the same insights that professional analysts use - before they write their own commentary. This guide walks you through every section of the official report.

1. The summary paragraph The report opens with three key numbers: total equity turnover, the number of shares traded, and the number of deals. These three figures tell you at a glance how active the market was.

Turnover above TZS 3 billion signals a busy day; below TZS 1 billion is quiet.

Deals indicate participation. Many deals with moderate turnover mean broad retail activity. Few deals with huge turnover suggest institutional block trades.

2. Block trade announcements Large, pre-arranged trades are reported separately. If you see a line like “CRDB counter traded a block of 58,000,000 shares”, you know a major transfer of ownership has occurred, but outside the normal order book. This explains sudden spikes in turnover without corresponding price moves.

3. Normal board trading by counter For each stock that traded, the report gives:

Opening and closing prices: The first and last trade of the day. The difference gives you the daily change.

High and low: The range the stock traded in. A wide range suggests volatility or disagreement among buyers and sellers.

Weighted average price (VWAP): The average price paid across all trades, weighted by volume. This is a more reliable indicator of fair value than the closing price alone.

Volume and turnover: The number of shares and total value traded. High volume confirms price moves; low volume makes them suspect.

Deals: The number of separate transactions. A stock with many deals is more liquid.

Outstanding bids and offers: The total number of shares waiting to buy (bid) and sell (offer) at the close. A deep bid side indicates buying interest; a heavy offer side suggests selling pressure.

4. ETF board Similar data is provided for the IEACLC-ETF and VERTEX-ETF. Watch for unusually large volumes, which can indicate institutional rotation.

5. Bond board The report lists government and corporate bonds that traded, with their coupons, maturities, face values, prices, and yields. This section tells you where the big, patient money is flowing - into long-term fixed income.

6. Market indicators page This is the summary of the entire market’s health.

Total and domestic market capitalisation: The combined value of all listed companies. A rising market cap means wealth is being created.

Indices: DSEI (All Share), TSI (Tanzania-only), BI (banks), IA (industrials), CS (commercial services). These tell you which sectors are driving the market.

Exchange rates: TZS/USD, TZS/KE, TZS/GBP. Important for importers, exporters, and diaspora investors.

7. Foreign flow table Shows what percentage of turnover was bought and sold by foreign investors. High foreign selling can pressure prices; high foreign buying can lift them. This is one of the most important pages for understanding the market’s mood.

How to use the report alongside the Daily DSE Wrap The official report gives you the raw numbers. Our Daily DSE Wrap gives you the story behind them. By reading both, you will develop the ability to form your own independent view - the mark of a truly confident investor.

Put It Into Practice Open today’s DSE market report and our Daily DSE Wrap side by side. Identify one data point in the report that we used in the Wrap. Write down how we interpreted it. In time, you will start making those connections yourself.
