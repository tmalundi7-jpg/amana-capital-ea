hat Is Fundamental Analysis? The Art of Finding True Value

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You now understand the economy, companies, and the marketplace. It’s time to put it all together. Fundamental analysis is the process of evaluating a company’s intrinsic value by examining its financial statements, competitive position, industry, and the broader economy. If the intrinsic value is significantly higher than the current market price, the stock may be undervalued — a buying opportunity. If the market price is far above intrinsic value, it may be overvalued — a reason to sell or avoid.

The Core Principle: Price vs. Value

The father of fundamental analysis, Benjamin Graham, taught that in the short run the market is a voting machine (popularity), but in the long run it is a weighing machine (substance). Your job is to estimate the substance — the true worth of the business — and compare it to the current market price.

Intrinsic value is not a single number. It’s a range, based on reasonable assumptions about future cash flows, growth, and risk. Different analysts will arrive at different estimates, but the discipline of trying to calculate it keeps you from overpaying.

The Two Main Approaches

Valuation multiples (relative valuation): Compare the company’s price to its earnings, book value, or sales, and then compare those multiples to peers or the company’s own history. We cover this in Article 4.2.

Discounted cash flow (DCF) (absolute valuation): Project the company’s future free cash flows and discount them back to today’s shillings using an appropriate discount rate. This is more complex but forces you to think deeply about the business.

For most DSE investors, relative valuation using simple multiples is a practical starting point.

The Fundamental Analysis Checklist

When we analyse a stock in the Daily DSE Wrap, we implicitly use this framework:

Understand the business: What does the company do? How does it make money?

Assess the industry: Is the sector growing? What’s the competitive landscape?

Examine the financials: Revenue and profit growth, margins, debt levels, cash flow.

Evaluate management: Are they competent and shareholder‑friendly?

Calculate valuation: Is the stock cheap or expensive relative to its history and peers?

Consider macro risks: Interest rates, exchange rates, regulation.
