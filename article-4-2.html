ey Valuation Ratios Made Simple (P/E, P/B, Dividend Yield)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Ratios are the shorthand of fundamental analysis. They allow you to compare companies of different sizes and to assess whether a stock is cheap or expensive at a glance. This article explains the three most widely used ratios on the DSE, with concrete examples.

Price‑to‑Earnings Ratio (P/E)

Formula: P/E = Share Price / Earnings per Share (EPS)

The P/E tells you how many years of current earnings it would take to recoup the purchase price, assuming earnings remain constant. A high P/E suggests investors expect strong future growth; a low P/E may signal a bargain — or a company in trouble.

On the DSE, banks often trade at P/E ratios between 5 and 12, while consumer companies may command higher multiples due to more predictable earnings. For example, if CRDB earns TZS 250 per share and trades at TZS 2,590, its P/E is about 10.4. That’s reasonable for a stable bank in a growing economy.

Watch for: Extraordinary items that distort earnings. Use “normalised” or “core” EPS if available, or simply look at the trend over several years.

Price‑to‑Book Ratio (P/B)

Formula: P/B = Share Price / Book Value per Share

Book value is the company’s assets minus liabilities — essentially its net worth per share. A P/B below 1.0 means the market values the company at less than its accounting net worth, which might indicate a bargain or a company that is destroying value. Banks are often analysed using P/B because their assets (loans) and liabilities (deposits) are marked to market or relatively transparent.

If NMB trades at TZS 16,160 and has a book value per share of, say, TZS 10,000, its P/B is 1.62. That premium suggests the market expects NMB to generate returns well above its book value.

Dividend Yield

Formula: Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Share Price × 100%

This tells you the cash return you receive just for holding the stock. In an environment where bank savings accounts offer 2–3%, a 5%+ dividend yield from a stable company is highly attractive.

On 29 June, TBL traded at TZS 9,710 and paid an annual dividend of TZS 818, giving a yield of 8.4%. That’s one reason TBL is widely held by income‑focused investors. Remember: dividends are not guaranteed. A high yield can be a trap if the company cannot sustain the payout.

Using Ratios Wisely

No single ratio should decide your investment. Compare a company’s ratios to:

Its own 5‑year history (is it becoming more or less expensive?).

Its industry peers (is it undervalued relative to competitors?).

The overall market (is the DSE cheap or expensive right now?).

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we occasionally reference valuation context — like “the banking sector trades at a discount to regional peers on a price‑to‑book basis.” That’s a macro observation built from these micro ratios.

Action step: For three DSE stocks you follow, look up their current P/E, P/B, and dividend yield. Write them down. Compare them to a regional peer (e.g., KCB on the NSE). What do the numbers suggest about relative value?
