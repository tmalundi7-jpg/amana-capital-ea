ow to Analyse a Sector: Industrials & Consumer Goods

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Tanzania’s industrial and consumer goods sector includes some of the DSE’s most recognisable names: TBL, TCC, TPCC, TOL, and TCCL (cigarette manufacturer). These companies make things — beer, cement, oil products — and their fortunes are tied to the physical economy. This article explains how to analyse them.

The Industrial Business Model

Industrial companies take raw materials (limestone for cement, barley and hops for beer, crude oil for fuel) and transform them into finished products. They earn profits by selling at a price above the cost of production. Key drivers:

Capacity utilisation: How much of the factory’s potential output is actually being produced. Higher utilisation spreads fixed costs over more units.

Input costs: Raw materials, energy, and transport are major expenses. A spike in global oil prices, for example, squeezes TOL’s margins.

Pricing power: The ability to pass cost increases to customers. TBL’s brand strength gives it significant pricing power.

Demand: Driven by population growth, urbanisation, and economic expansion. More construction means more cement demand (TCC, TPCC). More celebrations mean more beer (TBL).

Key Metrics for Industrials

Metric Why It Matters

Revenue growth Are sales volumes rising, or is growth only from price increases?

Gross margin Reveals pricing power and cost control. A stable or rising gross margin is positive.

Operating margin Reflects efficiency in running factories and distribution.

Return on capital employed (ROCE) Measures how well the company uses its capital (factories, machinery) to generate profit. Higher is better.

Debt‑to‑equity Heavy industry often requires large debt to build plants. Ensure the debt load is serviceable.

Capacity expansion plans New factories or production lines signal future growth but involve execution risk.

Consumer Goods Specifics

Consumer goods companies — brewers, cigarette makers, food processors — depend on brand loyalty, distribution reach, and volume. Key additional factors:

Market share: Is the company gaining or losing share?

Product mix: Are consumers moving to premium products (higher margin) or value lines?

Excise tax sensitivity: Brewing and cigarette companies are heavily taxed. A budget change can significantly impact earnings.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, when we mention that TBL volumes held up despite a price increase, we are observing pricing power in action. When we note that the government has raised excise duties, we are flagging a potential earnings headwind.

The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Industrial & Allied (IA) index is reported daily. Movements in IA often reflect news about infrastructure spending, commodity prices, or budget tax changes. When you see the IA index move, you can now diagnose which sub‑factors are at play.

Action step: Compare the gross margins of TBL and TCC over three years. Which company has more consistent pricing power? Why?
