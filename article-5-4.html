ehavioural Finance: Understanding Your Own Worst Enemy

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You can master macroeconomic cycles, financial statements, and valuation techniques, and still fail as an investor if you cannot master your own emotions. Behavioural finance studies the psychological biases that cause investors to make irrational decisions. This article covers the most dangerous biases and how to counter them.

Loss Aversion: The Pain of Losing Is Twice as Powerful as the Joy of Winning

People feel the pain of a loss much more intensely than the pleasure of an equivalent gain. This leads to two costly mistakes:

Holding onto losers too long, hoping they will recover, instead of cutting losses.

Selling winners too early, to lock in a gain and avoid the pain of seeing it disappear.

The fix: Have a predetermined thesis for every investment. If the thesis is broken, sell regardless of gain or loss. If the thesis is intact, hold through the noise.

Herd Mentality: Everyone’s Doing It, So It Must Be Right

When a stock is soaring and everyone is talking about it, the temptation to buy is overwhelming. Conversely, when the market is crashing and panic is everywhere, the urge to sell is almost irresistible. Herd behaviour creates bubbles and crashes.

The fix: Use a checklist (Article 4.7) and a long‑term plan. When you feel the herd’s pull, re‑read your written investment policy.

Overconfidence: I Know What Will Happen Next

After a few successful trades, many investors begin to believe they have a special talent for prediction. They trade more frequently, take larger positions, and eventually suffer disproportionate losses.

The fix: Track your decisions. Compare your actual returns to the market index. If you are not beating the DSEI over a full cycle, consider a simpler, more diversified approach.

Confirmation Bias: Seeking Information That Supports Your View

You buy a stock, and suddenly you notice only the positive news. You ignore or dismiss negative information. This bias prevents you from objectively reassessing your investments.

The fix: Actively seek out the bear case for every stock you own. Read critical analysis. If you can’t find any, you haven’t looked hard enough.

The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Daily Wrap is designed to counter behavioural biases. It presents facts calmly, focuses on the long term, avoids emotional language, and never hypes a stock. Use it as your anchor. When your emotions are screaming “buy!” or “sell!”, re‑read the day’s analysis and ask: What are the facts?

Action step: Reflect on your last investment decision. Which bias might have been at play? Write it down. Awareness is the first defence.
