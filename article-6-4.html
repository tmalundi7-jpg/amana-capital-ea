sing Collective Investment Schemes (UTT AMIS, etc.)

By Amana Capital East Africa � Investor Education Series

Not everyone has the time, expertise, or capital to build a diversified portfolio of individual stocks. Collective investment schemes (CIS) � also called unit trusts or mutual funds � offer an alternative. You pool your money with other investors, and a professional fund manager invests it on your behalf. In Tanzania, UTT AMIS is one of the primary providers.

How a Unit Trust Works

You buy units in the fund at the prevailing unit price (Net Asset Value per unit, or NAV).

The fund invests in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or both.

Returns come from capital gains on the underlying securities and interest/dividend income.

You can buy and redeem units on any dealing day.

Advantages of Unit Trusts

Instant diversification even with small amounts.

Professional management � the fund manager conducts the analysis and trading.

Liquidity � you can usually redeem units relatively quickly.

Regulatory oversight � CIS are regulated by CMSA, providing a layer of protection.

Types of Funds Available

Equity funds: Invest primarily in stocks. Higher risk, higher potential return.

Bond funds: Invest in government and corporate bonds. Lower risk, stable income.

Balanced funds: Mix of equities and bonds.

Money market funds: Very low risk, highly liquid, often used as a cash management tool.

How to Invest in UTT AMIS or Another CIS

Visit the fund manager�s office or website.

Complete an application form and provide KYC documents (ID, TIN, photo).

Deposit the minimum investment amount (often TZS 100,000 or less).

Receive a statement showing your unit holdings.

Monitor the fund�s performance and add to your investment regularly.

The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When we analyse the market, we often mention the bond fund or equity fund activity because these institutional players are significant market participants. Understanding CIS helps you interpret that flow. Additionally, if you are not yet ready to pick individual stocks, a CIS can be a sensible first step while you continue your education.

Action step: Research two CIS providers in Tanzania. Compare their fees, minimum investments, and historical performance. Consider allocating a small portion of your portfolio to a bond or balanced fund as a diversifier.

Step 1: Start with the Market Snapshot � What is the regime?

The snapshot gives you four critical data points: the DSEI (All Share Index), TSI (Tanzania Share Index), equity turnover, and the number of deals. Ask yourself:

Is turnover above TZS 3 billion? If yes, the market is liquid and active. If turnover is very low (below TZS 1.5 billion), large orders can push prices around, so treat small moves with caution.

Are deals high relative to volume? Many deals with moderate volume = broad retail participation ? the move is democratic, not one-off. Few deals with massive volume = institutional block trades ? the headline turnover overstates genuine market direction.

Which index is leading? DSEI up strongly with TSI lagging suggests the cross-listed stocks (KCB, NMG) are driving gains; perhaps it�s a regional story, not a domestic one.

Step 2: Foreign Flow � The mood of international capital

The Wrap tells you what percentage of turnover was foreign buying and selling. Since late 2024, foreign selling often dominated. Now, low foreign selling (below 5%) indicates that the foreign exit is over, and local demand is in control. If you see a sudden jump in foreign buying to 15%+, it may signal that international investors have turned positive � a potential catalyst for a broader rally.

Step 3: Top Movers � Where is the action?

The gainers and losers table tells you which stocks are attracting the most attention. Don�t just look at the percentage changes; cross-check with volume. A small stock can rise 10% on a handful of shares � that�s noise. A large-cap like CRDB or NMB moving 2-3% on high volume is significant.

Step 4: In Focus � The day�s story

Here we zoom in on one company or sector. Pay attention to the why � earnings, block trades, regulatory news. This is where you learn the fundamental reasoning that you can apply to your own watchlist.

Step 5: Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework � The long view

This section is always separated by a border; it is deliberately not a recommendation. It asks: what would a patient, 5-year allocator think about this? Use it to check your own long-term thesis. 2.2 New Article: Navigating DSE Liquidity � Small-Cap and Illiquid Stocks

By Amana Capital East Africa � Investor Education Series

Liquidity is the ability to buy or sell an asset quickly without significantly moving the price. On the DSE, liquidity varies dramatically. CRDB and NMB often trade hundreds of thousands of shares daily; some counters trade only a few hundred in a week. Ignoring liquidity is one of the fastest ways to lose money. This article explains how to handle it.

Understanding DSE liquidity tiers

Liquidity Tier Examples What it means for you

High (blue-chip) CRDB, NMB, TBL, VODA, KCB You can usually trade a few hundred shares at or near the quoted price. Spreads are narrow.

Moderate TCC, TPCC, TOL, SWIS, NICO You may need patience. Orders of more than 50-100 shares could move the price.

Low (small-cap) DCB, MUCOBA, MBP, PAL, TTP A single order of a few thousand shillings can change the price by several percent. Spreads are wide.

The cost of illiquidity: the bid-ask spread

The spread is the gap between what buyers are willing to pay (bid) and what sellers are asking (offer). On a liquid stock, the spread might be 0.1-0.5%. On an illiquid stock, it can be 5-10% or more. That means if you buy at the offer and immediately sell at the bid, you lose the spread. You must overcome this built-in loss before you can make a profit.

Practical rules for illiquid counters

Use limit orders only. Never place a market order on a low-volume stock; you might fill at a price far from the last quote.

Trade smaller sizes. If daily volume is 2,000 shares, don�t try to buy 5,000 in one go. Break it into multiple orders over days.

Be patient. Your order may remain unfilled for days or weeks. That�s normal.

Check the outstanding bids and offers. In the DSE daily price table, you can see how many shares are waiting at the bid and offer. If there are zero bids, do not buy � you won�t be able to sell later without taking a loss.

Liquidity and valuation

Illiquid stocks often appear cheap based on P/E or P/B, but that �discount� is the market charging you for illiquidity. Don�t mistake a low price for a bargain. Always ask: If I needed to sell tomorrow, could I do so without losing a large chunk of my capital?

When illiquidity works for you

If you are a true long-term investor with a small amount of capital, an illiquid stock bought at a very low price can occasionally produce outsized returns when a catalyst (a dividend, a new investor, an earnings surprise) brings attention and volume. But this is speculation, not investment. Only allocate money you are prepared to lose entirely.

2.3 Case Study: CRDB Bank � A Fundamental Walk-Through

By Amana Capital East Africa � Investor Education Series

This article applies the complete Amana Capital EA Stock Analysis Checklist (Article 4.7) to a real DSE company: CRDB Bank Plc. Use it as a model for analysing any holding.

Macro Context (Module 1)

As of June 2026, Tanzania�s economy is growing at 5-6%, inflation is moderate, and the Bank of Tanzania has kept the policy rate stable. This is supportive for banking: loan demand is rising, credit costs are manageable, and the exchange rate is relatively stable. The macroeconomic backdrop is favourable for well-capitalised banks.

Industry Analysis (Article 4.3)

CRDB operates in the banking sector, which is the largest component of the DSE by market capitalisation. The sector benefits from financial inclusion tailwinds, mobile banking adoption, and an expanding middle class. Competitors include NMB, KCB (cross-listed), and smaller domestic banks. CRDB holds the largest branch network, which is a competitive moat, though NMB has a strong mobile and retail franchise.

Business Model & Moat

CRDB�s business model is traditional commercial banking: taking deposits and lending to businesses, individuals, and the government. Its moat lies in:

Distribution: Over 250 branches nationwide, far more than most rivals.

Brand: Decades of presence and a reputation as a stable, government-linked institution.

Switching costs: Corporate clients with multiple accounts, loans, and payroll services face significant friction to move.

Financial Health (Modules 2.2�2.4)

Based on recent annual reports (illustrative):

Revenue growth: Low double-digit, driven by loan book expansion and fee income from mobile banking.

Net Interest Margin (NIM): ~6%, consistent with the sector average.

Return on Equity (ROE): ~16-18%, indicating efficient use of shareholder capital.

Non-Performing Loans (NPLs): ~4.5%, slightly above some peers but within the BOT�s comfort range.

Capital Adequacy Ratio: Well above the regulatory minimum, providing capacity for further lending.

Dividend: Has paid a regular dividend, though the payout ratio is moderate � management retains earnings for growth.

Management Quality

Management has pursued a steady, conservative strategy. Communication in annual reports is clear. There have been no significant governance red flags. The bank has a diversified loan book, avoiding excessive concentration in any single sector.

Valuation (Article 4.2)

Assume the following (illustrative, based on recent data):

Share price: TZS 2,590

Earnings per share (EPS): ~TZS 250

P/E ratio: 10.4

Book value per share: ~TZS 1,450

P/B ratio: 1.79

Dividend yield: ~3.5%

Compared to regional peers (e.g., KCB trades at a P/B of ~1.0-1.2 on the NSE), CRDB appears slightly more expensive on a book basis, but its higher ROE and growth potential may justify the premium. Historically, CRDB�s P/E has ranged from 7 to 14, so 10.4 is in the middle � not a screaming bargain, but not overvalued either.

Technical Snapshot

A simple chart shows CRDB has been in a gradual uptrend since early 2026, with support around 2,500 and resistance near 2,650. Volume on up days has been healthy, suggesting accumulation rather than a short-term spike.

Conclusion

Under the Amana Capital EA Checklist, CRDB presents a case of a strong, moated bank with solid financials, capable management, and a reasonable valuation � but not a deep-value opportunity. A long-horizon investor might hold or accumulate on dips near 2,500, while a disciplined seller might trim if the P/E exceeds 14 without a corresponding earnings increase.
