Navigating DSE Liquidity: Small‑Cap and Illiquid Stocks

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Liquidity is the ability to buy or sell an asset quickly without significantly moving the price. On the DSE, liquidity varies dramatically. CRDB and NMB often trade hundreds of thousands of shares daily; some counters trade only a few hundred in a week. Ignoring liquidity is one of the fastest ways to lose money.

Understanding DSE Liquidity Tiers

  • High (blue‑chip): CRDB, NMB, TBL, VODA, KCB. You can usually trade a few hundred shares near the quoted price. Spreads are narrow.
  • Moderate: TCC, TPCC, TOL, SWIS, NICO. You may need patience; orders of more than 50–100 shares could move the price.
  • Low (small‑cap): DCB, MUCOBA, MBP, PAL, TTP. A single order of a few thousand shillings can change the price by several percent. Spreads are wide.

The Cost of Illiquidity: The Bid‑Ask Spread

The spread is the gap between what buyers will pay (bid) and what sellers ask (offer). On liquid stocks, it might be 0.1–0.5%; on illiquid stocks, 5–10% or more. If you buy at the offer and immediately sell at the bid, you lose the spread. You must overcome this built‑in loss before making a profit.

Practical Rules for Illiquid Counters

  • Use limit orders only. Never place a market order on a low‑volume stock.
  • Trade smaller sizes. If daily volume is 2,000 shares, don’t try to buy 5,000 in one go.
  • Be patient. Your order may remain unfilled for days or weeks. That’s normal.
  • Check outstanding bids and offers. If there are zero bids, do not buy — you won’t be able to sell later without taking a loss.

Liquidity and Valuation

Illiquid stocks often appear cheap on P/E or P/B, but that “discount” is the market charging you for illiquidity. Don’t mistake a low price for a bargain. Always ask: If I needed to sell tomorrow, could I do so without losing a large chunk of my capital?
