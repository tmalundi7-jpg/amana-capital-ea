Article 7.4 - Corporate Actions Beyond Dividends: Rights Issues, Splits, and Bonus Shares

By Amana Capital East Africa - Investor Education Series

Dividends are the most common corporate action, but companies also use other mechanisms that affect your holdings. Understanding them is critical.

Rights Issue A company offers existing shareholders the right to buy additional shares at a discount to the market price, in proportion to their current holding (e.g., 1 new share for every 5 held). This raises fresh capital for the company. If you participate, you maintain your ownership percentage and buy at a discount. If you do not, your ownership is diluted (your percentage of the company shrinks), and the value of your existing shares may fall because more shares now exist. Rights are usually renounceable - you can sell your rights in the market if you choose not to take up the offer.

Bonus Issue (Scrip Issue) Also called a capitalisation issue, a bonus issue gives existing shareholders additional shares for free, in proportion to their holding (e.g., 1 bonus share for every 10 held). No new capital is raised; the company simply converts reserves (retained earnings) into share capital. Your ownership percentage does not change, and the share price typically adjusts downward to reflect the increased number of shares, leaving the total value of your holding unchanged. However, bonus issues can improve liquidity and signal management confidence.

Share Split A company divides its existing shares into multiple shares (e.g., a 2-for-1 split doubles the number of shares and halves the price). The total value of your holding stays the same. Splits are often done to make shares more affordable and improve liquidity, which can attract more investors.

Mergers and Acquisitions When two companies combine, shareholders of the acquired company may receive cash, shares in the acquiring company, or a mix. These events can create or destroy value depending on the terms. Always evaluate whether the offer fairly values your shares.

What to Do When a Corporate Action Is Announced

Read the circular from the company carefully.

Understand the impact on your ownership and the share price.

For rights issues, decide whether to participate, sell your rights, or do nothing (the last option usually leads to dilution).

Check the record date - you must hold shares before this date to be entitled.

If uncertain, consult your broker or a financial advisor.
