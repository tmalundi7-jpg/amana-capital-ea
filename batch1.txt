In the previous article, we learned that the economy is the sum of all transactions, and that its growth or contraction shapes the environment in which every company operates. But who controls the temperature of that environment? Who decides whether money flows freely or becomes expensive and scarce?

The answer is the Bank of Tanzania (BOT) — our central bank. Understanding what the BOT does, why it makes the decisions it makes, and how those decisions reach your investment portfolio is one of the most powerful advantages an investor can have. This article explains it in plain language, with practical examples from the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange.
1. What Is a Central Bank, and What Does It Want?

Every country has a central bank. In Tanzania, it’s the Bank of Tanzania, established by the Bank of Tanzania Act. Think of the BOT as the guardian of the financial system. Its primary job is to:

    Keep inflation under control — so the money in your pocket keeps its value.

    Maintain a stable currency — so businesses and investors can plan.

    Supervise commercial banks — so your deposits are safe.

    Manage the country’s foreign reserves — so we can pay for imports and service external debt.

For investors, the single most important function is monetary policy — the art of controlling how much money circulates in the economy and at what price.
2. The Price of Money: The Benchmark Interest Rate

Money, just like maize or fuel, has a price. The price of money is the interest rate. When you borrow from a bank, you pay interest. When you deposit savings, you earn interest. The BOT sets a benchmark rate (called the Central Bank Rate, or CBR) that influences all other interest rates in the economy.

When the BOT lowers the CBR:

    Commercial banks can borrow from the BOT more cheaply.

    They, in turn, lower the rates they charge businesses and individuals.

    Borrowing increases. Companies expand, consumers spend, and economic activity picks up.

    On the DSE, this is generally positive for stocks — especially banks, whose loan books grow, and consumer companies, whose customers now have more to spend.

When the BOT raises the CBR:

    Borrowing becomes expensive.

    Businesses delay expansion, consumers cut spending.

    Stock prices often fall because future profits are now discounted at a higher rate, and economic growth slows.

So, the BOT’s interest rate decision ripples directly into the share prices you track daily.

Tanzania in focus: In recent years, the BOT has kept the CBR relatively stable, often in the range of 5–6%. This stability has encouraged long‑term capital allocation into equities and bonds. In our Daily DSE Wrap on 29 June 2026, we noted that the banking index (BI) reached 19,528.67 — a level that reflects, in part, a stable rate environment that allows banks to lend profitably.
3. How the BOT Controls Money Supply

The BOT does not just set an interest rate and walk away. It actively manages liquidity through several tools:

    Treasury bill and bond auctions: The BOT issues government securities on behalf of the Treasury. When it wants to reduce money in circulation (to fight inflation), it sells more securities, pulling cash out of the banking system. When it wants to inject cash, it buys back securities or reduces the amount it sells.

    Reserve requirements: Commercial banks must keep a portion of their deposits at the BOT. Raising this requirement means banks have less to lend; lowering it frees up funds.

    Open market operations: The BOT buys or sells securities in the secondary market to fine‑tune liquidity day‑to‑day.

For a DSE investor, the most visible of these is the bond market. When the 29 June 2026 DSE report showed TZS 16.20 billion in bond trades, much of that activity was driven by institutional investors reacting to the BOT’s monetary stance. A heavy bond auction can drain cash that might otherwise have found its way into equities — a dynamic we often discuss in the Daily Wrap under “Rotation Signals.”

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

In the previous articles, we learned how the economy grows and how the Bank of Tanzania controls the price of money. Now we examine a force that can undo all your hard work without a single headline: inflation. It doesn’t crash markets; it slowly, quietly eats away the purchasing power of your savings and your investment returns. For a Tanzanian investor, understanding inflation is not optional — it’s the foundation of every real return calculation.
1. What Is Inflation?

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises over time. When inflation is 5% per year, a basket of goods that cost TZS 10,000 last year costs TZS 10,500 this year. Your money has lost purchasing power.

In Tanzania, the National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) measures inflation using the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which tracks a fixed basket of commonly purchased items — food, housing, transport, clothing, and so on. The headline inflation figure you hear in the news is the year‑on‑year change in that CPI.
2. Why Inflation Matters to an Investor

Imagine you keep TZS 1,000,000 in a savings account earning 2% interest. In a year, you have TZS 1,020,000. But if inflation ran at 7% over the same period, the things you could buy for TZS 1,000,000 a year ago now cost TZS 1,070,000. Your “gain” is actually a loss of TZS 50,000 in real terms.

The same logic applies to stocks and bonds. If a company’s earnings grow by 8% but inflation is 6%, the real growth is only about 2%. If a government bond pays a 14% coupon but inflation spikes to 15%, your real return is negative.

The goal of every investor is not just to make money, but to earn a return that beats inflation after taxes. This is the “real return” mindset.
3. How Inflation Moves Through the Economy and Hits Different DSE Sectors

Inflation does not affect all companies equally. Some can pass rising costs to customers; others cannot. Here’s how it typically plays out on the DSE:
Sector	Impact of Rising Inflation
Banks	Mixed: higher inflation often leads to higher interest rates, which can boost net interest margins — but only if loan demand stays healthy. If inflation runs too high, loan defaults increase.
Consumer staples (brewers, telcos)	Moderately protected: people still buy beer and airtime, but they may trade down to cheaper options. Companies with strong brands (like TBL) can raise prices without losing customers.
Cement & construction (TCC, TPCC)	Vulnerable: rising input costs (energy, transport) squeeze margins, and if the central bank hikes rates to fight inflation, construction lending dries up.
Bonds	Severely impacted: existing fixed‑coupon bonds lose value when inflation rises because new bonds offer higher yields. Bondholders see paper losses.
Gold & commodities	Often benefit: gold is traditionally seen as an inflation hedge, though Tanzania’s listed miners are limited.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we note inflationary pressures when they appear — for example, if the BOT signals concern about rising food prices, we would flag that consumer‑facing stocks could face margin pressure.
4. The BOT’s Role: Fighting Inflation with Interest Rates

As we covered in Article 1.2, the Bank of Tanzania’s primary weapon against inflation is the Central Bank Rate (CBR). When inflation rises above the BOT’s target (typically around 5%), it will raise the CBR. Higher rates cool the economy by making borrowing more expensive, which reduces spending and eventually slows price rises.

For an equity investor, this creates a difficult trade‑off: higher rates protect the purchasing power of the shilling, but they also increase the discount rate applied to future corporate earnings, which can push stock prices lower in the short term. The key is to distinguish between temporary inflation (caused by a drought or a fuel price shock) and entrenched inflation (caused by excessive money printing or a wage‑price spiral). Temporary inflation often reverses without drastic rate hikes; entrenched inflation forces the BOT to act decisively.
5. Inflation in Tanzania: What the Data Tells Us

Tanzania has generally enjoyed moderate inflation in the 3–6% range over the past several years, a stark contrast to the double‑digit inflation of the early 2010s. This stability is one reason the DSE All Share Index (DSEI) has trended higher: investors are not panicked by runaway prices, and companies can plan with reasonable confidence.

However, investors must always watch for shocks:

    Food prices: Tanzania is an agricultural economy. A drought can send maize prices soaring, which feeds directly into the CPI and reduces rural incomes.

    Fuel prices: Oil is imported, so global crude prices directly affect transport and manufacturing costs.

    Exchange rate pass‑through: A weakening shilling makes imports more expensive, which can stoke inflation.

We monitor these channels in our Daily DSE Wrap. When the DSEI moves sharply, the explanation is often found in one of these inflationary or deflationary pressures.
6. How to Inflation‑Proof Your Portfolio

No portfolio can be completely immune to inflation, but you can take practical steps:

    Favour companies with pricing power — those that sell essential products and can raise prices without losing customers. Breweries, mobile networks, and dominant banks often fit this category.

    Include dividend‑paying stocks — companies that can maintain or grow dividends during inflationary periods provide a cash return that helps offset rising living costs.

    Consider inflation‑linked bonds — the Tanzanian government occasionally issues bonds whose coupon or principal adjusts with inflation. These offer direct protection.

    Stay diversified — equities, bonds, real estate, and cash each respond differently to inflation. A well‑built portfolio balances these exposures.

    Reinvest dividends and interest — compounding at a rate that beats inflation is the most reliable way to build real wealth over time.

7. From Macro to Market: The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In every Daily DSE Wrap, we report the day’s market snapshot, but we also pay attention to the macro context. When we note that bond turnover is surging while equities are flat, it might signal that large investors are moving money into fixed income because they expect higher inflation and therefore higher interest rates. When we highlight a rally in banking stocks, it might reflect a stable inflation outlook that supports steady loan growth.

    Next time you read the Daily Wrap, ask: What is the inflation story today, and how might it explain the sector moves I’m seeing?

    If you want to dive deeper into how to evaluate a specific bank’s ability to handle inflation, proceed to our article “How to Analyse a Sector: Banking” (Module 4.3).

Key Takeaway

Inflation is the silent thief of wealth. It does not announce itself with a market crash, but it steadily erodes the value of every shilling you hold. The only defence is to invest in assets that can grow faster than prices rise. Understanding inflation — and how the BOT fights it — equips you to make decisions that preserve and grow your real purchasing power over the long term.

Article 1.4 – Exchange Rates and Your Investments

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You may never have traded a single US dollar, but if you own shares in a Tanzanian company or are a diaspora investor sending money home, the exchange rate between the Tanzanian shilling (TZS) and other currencies directly affects your returns. This article explains how exchange rates work, why they move, and how they ripple through your portfolio — with practical, DSE‑focused examples.
1. What Is an Exchange Rate?

An exchange rate is simply the price of one country’s currency expressed in another. For example, if USD/TZS = 2,628.10, that means one US dollar buys 2,628.10 Tanzanian shillings. When the shilling weakens, the number goes up (more shillings per dollar). When the shilling strengthens, the number goes down.
2. What Moves Exchange Rates?

In the long run, exchange rates reflect the relative economic strength and inflation differentials between countries. In the short run, they are driven by:

    Trade flows: When Tanzania exports more than it imports (gold, cashews, tourism, services), foreign currency flows in, strengthening the shilling. When imports exceed exports, the shilling weakens.

    Interest rate differentials: If the Bank of Tanzania offers higher interest rates than the US Federal Reserve or the Bank of England, foreign investors may move money into Tanzanian bonds to capture the higher yield. This demand for shillings can strengthen the currency. Conversely, if foreign investors pull money out, the shilling falls.

    Foreign aid and FDI: Large inflows of development aid or foreign direct investment can support the shilling. Outflows or reduced aid can weaken it.

    Market sentiment: Political stability, regulatory predictability, and global risk appetite all affect whether foreigners want to hold shilling‑denominated assets.

In the 29 June 2026 DSE report, the exchange rate stood at TZS/USD 2,628.10 and TZS/GBP 3,474.09. These numbers matter because they tell you the purchasing power of the shilling on the global stage — and they influence the profitability of many DSE‑listed companies.
3. How Exchange Rates Hit Different DSE Stocks

The impact of a stronger or weaker shilling depends on whether a company is a net importer, a net exporter, or domestically focused.
Type of Company	Effect of a Weaker Shilling (TZS depreciates)	Effect of a Stronger Shilling (TZS appreciates)
Importers (manufacturers that rely on imported raw materials, fuel distributors, etc.)	Negative: Input costs rise in shilling terms, squeezing margins.	Positive: Cheaper imports boost margins.

4. The Exchange Rate and Inflation: The Pass‑Through Effect

There is a direct link between exchange rates and the inflation we discussed in Article 1.3. When the shilling weakens, imported goods — fuel, machinery, cooking oil, electronics — become more expensive. This “pass‑through” effect pushes up the CPI. The Bank of Tanzania then faces a dilemma: raise interest rates to fight inflation (which may further slow the economy) or allow the shilling to float and hope inflation subsides.

For an investor, this means that a sharp drop in the shilling can trigger a chain reaction: higher inflation → potential rate hikes → lower equity valuations → possible selling pressure on imported‑input companies. This chain is exactly the type of macro‑to‑market logic we apply in the Daily DSE Wrap when interpreting foreign flow data.
5. Diaspora Investing: Making Exchange Rates Work for You

If you are a Tanzanian living in the UK, Europe, or the USA, you are in a unique position: you earn in a hard currency and invest in a frontier market. Exchange rate movements can amplify your returns or cushion your losses.

    A weaker shilling works in your favour when you are buying in: your pounds or dollars convert to more shillings, allowing you to purchase more shares for the same amount of foreign currency.

    A stronger shilling helps when you eventually sell and repatriate funds: you get more foreign currency for your shillings.

This dual advantage means diaspora investors can systematically accumulate DSE shares when the shilling is weak and benefit from both capital appreciation and potential currency gains when they exit — a strategy called “double compounding.”

Practical tip: Track the TZS/GBP and TZS/USD rates just as you track the DSEI. We publish the daily exchange rates in every DSE report and in the Daily DSE Wrap.

Article 1.5 – The Economic Cycle: When to Plant and When to Harvest

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Markets do not move in a straight line. Just as the seasons dictate when a farmer plants and when they harvest, the economy moves through a rhythm — expansion, peak, contraction, and recovery. This is the economic cycle. For an investor, understanding this cycle is like having a map: it tells you roughly where you are and which sectors are likely to thrive or struggle at that stage.
1. What Is the Economic Cycle?

The economic cycle is the natural fluctuation of economic activity around a long‑term growth trend. It has four phases:

    Expansion: GDP is growing. Businesses are hiring. Consumers are spending. Credit is readily available. Profits rise, and stock markets tend to climb. This phase can last for years.

    Peak: Growth reaches its maximum rate. The economy may be running at full capacity. Inflation often picks up. The central bank may start raising interest rates to prevent overheating. Stock markets become volatile as investors debate whether the good times can continue.

    Contraction (or recession): GDP growth slows or turns negative. Companies cut back, unemployment rises, and consumer spending falls. Stock markets usually decline, sometimes sharply.

    Recovery: The economy starts growing again. Confidence returns, credit eases, and businesses begin to expand. Stock markets often rebound before the official recovery is confirmed.

These phases are not perfectly regular, and their timing is notoriously difficult to predict. But they have repeated throughout economic history in every country — including Tanzania.
2. How Different Sectors Perform at Each Stage

Because different industries have different sensitivities to economic growth, the cycle creates a rotating cast of leaders and laggards. This is why a diversified portfolio tends to smooth returns, but it also offers an opportunity: you can tilt your investments toward sectors that historically do well in the current phase.
Phase	Sectors That Tend to Perform Well	Sectors That Tend to Lag
Early Expansion	Financials (banks, insurance), consumer discretionary (brewers, media), industrials	Defensive sectors like utilities (if available), bonds
Late Expansion / Peak	Energy, materials (cement, metals), technology	Interest‑rate‑sensitive sectors like real estate may start to struggle
Contraction / Recession	Consumer staples (food, beverages, telecoms), healthcare, utilities	Cyclical sectors: financials, industrials, luxury goods
Recovery	Cyclicals: financials, consumer discretionary, real estate	Safe havens like government bonds lose some appeal as risk appetite returns

On the DSE, the banking sector (BI index) often leads in early expansion, as we saw on 29 June 2026 when BI hit 19,528.67 — a sign of confidence in continued lending and economic activity. Consumer goods companies like TBL also benefit from rising disposable incomes. Meanwhile, in a contraction, defensive stocks like VODA (telecoms) might hold up better because people keep using their phones regardless of the economy.
3. Where Are We Now? Reading the Cycle in Tanzania

As of June 2026, Tanzania’s economy has been in a sustained expansion, with GDP growth around 5–6%. The DSEI has climbed from 2,353.86 a year ago to over 4,040. The banking sector is leading, and foreign selling has collapsed to minimal levels — a sign that local confidence is driving the market higher. This is consistent with a mid‑expansion phase, where growth is steady but not yet overheated, and inflation remains within the BOT’s comfort zone.

The danger in a mid‑expansion phase is complacency. Investors might assume growth will continue forever. But the cycle always turns. The Bank of Tanzania will eventually need to raise rates if inflation threatens, or an external shock could slow the economy. Part of being a disciplined, long‑horizon investor — as we advocate at Amana Capital EA — is preparing for the next phase even while enjoying the current one.
4. How to Position Your Portfolio Across the Cycle

You cannot time the cycle perfectly, and trying to jump in and out of sectors usually backfires. Instead, follow these principles:

    Stay diversified across sectors. Do not put all your money in banks just because they are leading now. Hold a mix of financials, industrials, consumer goods, and telecoms.

    Re‑balance annually. If one sector has outperformed and now makes up too much of your portfolio, trim it back and add to underweight sectors. This forces you to buy low and sell high without needing to predict the cycle.

    Keep some cash or bond exposure. A cash reserve allows you to buy equities during a contraction, when prices are depressed and bargains abound. This is one reason we discuss bond activity in the Daily DSE Wrap: a rotation from equities to bonds often signals that smart money is preparing for a slowdown.

    Focus on companies, not macro forecasts. Even in a recession, well‑managed companies with strong balance sheets can survive and emerge stronger. In an expansion, poorly run companies can fail. The cycle matters, but company quality matters more.

5. The Daily DSE Wrap as Your Cycle Compass

Every trading day, our Market Pulse section gives you the key cycle indicators:

    Equity turnover and deals: Rising turnover suggests expansion‑phase confidence. Falling turnover may signal caution.

    Foreign flow: High foreign buying often means international investors see more room for growth. High foreign selling may warn of a perceived peak or external shock.

    Bond activity: A surge in bond purchases can indicate a “risk‑off” shift — investors locking in fixed income in anticipation of slower growth.

    Sector indices: We track BI (banks), IA (industrials), and CS (commercial services). Their relative performance tells you which phase the market is pricing in.

By reading the Wrap with the economic cycle in mind, you move from observing numbers to understanding the deeper narrative they form.
6. From Macro to Market: The Direct Link

In Article 1.1 we learned that the economy is the sum of all transactions. The economic cycle simply describes how those transactions ebb and flow. When the cycle is in expansion, company earnings rise, and the DSEI climbs. When it contracts, the opposite occurs. You cannot control the cycle, but you can prepare for it, and you can use the Daily DSE Wrap to stay informed about its current state.

    Next time you read the Daily Wrap, ask: Which sectors are leading? Does this match what I would expect in the current phase of the cycle? If banks and industrials are both rising, it likely signals expansion. If telecoms and consumer staples are the only ones holding up, caution may be warranted.

Key Takeaway

The economic cycle is the farmer’s almanac for investors. It tells you when the ground is fertile and when a storm may be coming. You don’t need to predict the weather perfectly; you just need to know enough to plant the right seeds at the right time and to keep your harvest safe when conditions change.

### Article 1.6 – How Government Spending and National Debt Affect Markets

*By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series*

Every year, the Tanzanian government collects taxes, receives foreign aid, and borrows money to spend on roads, schools, hospitals, and defence. #### 1. Government Spending: The Fuel and the Drag

When the government spends money on infrastructure — a new railway, a port expansion, an electrification project — it pours cash into the economy. This is one reason we track bond turnover so closely in the Daily DSE Wrap: large bond issuance can suck liquidity out of the equity market.
