Article 1.6 – How Government Spending and National Debt Affect Markets

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Every year, the Tanzanian government collects taxes, receives foreign aid, and borrows money to spend on roads, schools, hospitals, and defence. The way it manages its finances — through fiscal policy — creates powerful currents that flow directly into the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange. This article explains those currents and teaches you how to read the budget as an investor.
1. Government Spending: The Fuel and the Drag

When the government spends money on infrastructure — a new railway, a port expansion, an electrification project — it pours cash into the economy. Construction companies earn revenue; they hire workers; those workers spend wages. The initial spending multiplies through the economy. Companies that provide cement, steel, and logistics — think Tanga Cement (TCC), Portland Cement (TPCC), and TOL — often see their order books swell.

But government spending must be paid for. If the government borrows aggressively to finance that spending, it can crowd out private borrowers. Banks may prefer to lend to the government (via Treasury bonds) rather than to businesses, especially if bond yields are attractive. This is one reason we track bond turnover so closely in the Daily DSE Wrap: large bond issuance can suck liquidity out of the equity market.

    Key takeaway: Infrastructure‑heavy budgets are generally positive for industrial and construction stocks, but watch the funding side — heavy borrowing can push up interest rates and pressure bank lending margins.

2. National Debt: When Borrowing Becomes a Concern

Tanzania’s national debt has grown in recent years, driven by development projects and pandemic‑era borrowing. For an investor, the level of debt matters because:

    High debt can lead to higher taxes on corporations and individuals, which reduces corporate earnings and disposable income.

    High debt can weaken the shilling if foreign investors fear default or currency depreciation, leading to capital flight.

    High debt can force the government to cut spending in other areas, which can hurt sectors that depend on public contracts.

However, not all debt is equal. Debt used to build productive assets — a port that boosts trade, a power plant that cuts electricity costs — can generate returns that more than cover the interest cost. As investors, we distinguish between productive debt and wasteful debt.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we note government bond auction results. When a bond is oversubscribed, it signals confidence in the government’s creditworthiness. When yields spike, it may signal nervousness about debt sustainability, which can ripple into the equity market.
3. The Budget Cycle: Dates Every DSE Investor Should Know

The Tanzanian fiscal year runs from July to June. The Minister of Finance presents the national budget in June, detailing spending plans, tax changes, and borrowing requirements. This speech is one of the most important events of the year for the stock market.

What to watch in the budget:

    Corporate tax changes — directly affect listed company earnings.

    Excise duties — changes on beer, cigarettes, fuel, and mobile services impact TBL, TCC, VODA, and others.

    Infrastructure allocations — signal which sectors will benefit from government contracts.

    Bond issuance plans — indicate how much the government will borrow, affecting bond yields and equity liquidity.

After the budget, the Daily DSE Wrap will analyse the implications sector by sector, so you don’t have to guess.
4. How to Integrate Fiscal Policy into Your Investment Process

    Read the budget speech (or at least our summary in the Daily Wrap). Identify the sectors that will gain from spending increases and those that will suffer from tax hikes.

    Track government bond yields. Rising yields may indicate that the government is borrowing more aggressively, which can compete with equities for investment capital.

    Monitor the national debt‑to‑GDP ratio. As a rule of thumb, a ratio below 50% is comfortable; above 70% can become problematic. Tanzania’s ratio is currently in the 40–45% range, which is manageable but worth watching.

5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

Every major fiscal event — budget speech, bond auction, tax announcement — receives dedicated coverage in the Daily Wrap. When you read our analysis of why the bond market moved or why construction stocks rallied, the explanation often traces back to a fiscal policy decision made weeks or months earlier. Understanding fiscal policy gives you the “why” behind the “what.”

    Next time you read the Daily Wrap, check if any government‑related news is mentioned. Ask: How does this spending or borrowing plan affect the sectors I hold?

Article 2.1 – Supply, Demand, and the Business That Sells It

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

In Module 1 we explored the economy at the macro level. Now we zoom in to the individual company. The most fundamental tool for understanding a business is the law of supply and demand — the same force that determines the price of onions in Kariakoo also determines the profits of TBL, VODA, and CRDB.
1. The Basics: What Drives Price and Quantity?

    Supply is how much of a product producers are willing to sell at a given price. Higher prices incentivise more supply.

    Demand is how much consumers are willing to buy at a given price. Lower prices attract more demand.

    Equilibrium is where the two curves meet. That’s the market price and quantity.

For a business, the goal is to sell at a price well above the cost of production. The difference is profit.
2. Pricing Power: The Investor’s Best Friend

Not all companies can raise prices without losing customers. A company with pricing power can increase prices steadily over time without significantly reducing demand. This power comes from:

    Strong brand: Consumers pay more for a trusted brand. TBL’s Safari Lager commands loyalty that a generic beer cannot.

    Monopoly or oligopoly position: When few competitors exist, pricing power is high. Tanzania’s cement industry has limited players, giving firms like TCC and TPCC some pricing ability.

    High switching costs: If a customer cannot easily move to a competitor, the company has power. A bank where a client holds a mortgage, salary account, and mobile banking profile is hard to leave.

Companies with pricing power are generally better investments because they can protect margins during inflationary periods and reinvest profits for growth.
3. How Demand Shifts and What It Means

Demand can shift for many reasons: rising incomes, changing tastes, population growth, or new technology. For example:

    Rising incomes: As Tanzania’s middle class expands, demand for financial services, mobile data, and branded consumer goods increases. This benefits banks like CRDB and NMB, and telcos like VODA.

    Demographic shifts: A young, urbanising population drives demand for housing (cement), entertainment, and mobile money.

    Regulatory changes: A new tax on mobile money transactions could reduce demand for that service, hitting telco revenues.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, when we note that a particular sector is rallying, demand‑side factors are often the underlying driver. We track turnover and volume because they reveal where demand is flowing in real time.
4. Supply‑Side Factors: What Can Go Wrong

Even great demand won’t help a company that can’t deliver. Supply disruptions — drought for agricultural inputs, power outages for manufacturers, import delays for raw materials — can crush margins. Companies that manage their supply chains well, diversify their suppliers, and maintain inventory buffers are more resilient.

When analysing a DSE company, ask:

    Where do its raw materials come from?

    How dependent is it on imports?

    Does it have a reliable and affordable supply of electricity and water?

5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

Every time we highlight a stock’s volume spike or price movement, supply and demand are at work. For example, on 29 June 2026, NMB traded 228,782 shares (including a block trade) — that’s demand meeting supply at a mutually agreeable price. The fact that the price held near TZS 16,160 tells you that sellers were not desperate and buyers were willing to pay.

    Action step: In today’s Daily Wrap, pick one stock that moved significantly. Ask: Was it a demand‑driven move or a supply‑driven move? What might have caused the shift?

Article 2.2 – How to Read a Company’s Financial Statements (Part 1 – The Income Statement)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Financial statements are the report card of a business. They tell you whether a company is healthy, growing, or in trouble. The first — and most watched — is the income statement, also called the profit and loss statement (P&L). It answers a simple question: Is this company making money?
1. The Anatomy of an Income Statement

Every income statement, from a kiosk to a multinational, follows this structure:
Line	What It Means
Revenue (Sales)	Total money earned from selling goods or services before any costs.
– Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)	Direct costs of producing what was sold (raw materials, labour, energy).
= Gross Profit	What’s left after covering direct production costs.
– Operating Expenses	Indirect costs: salaries, rent, marketing, administration, depreciation.
= Operating Profit (EBIT)	Profit from the core business before interest and taxes.
– Interest Expense	Cost of debt.
+ Interest Income	Earnings from cash or investments.
– Tax	Corporate income tax.
= Net Profit	The bottom line — what belongs to shareholders.

For a Tanzanian example, let’s look at a simplified CRDB income statement concept: the bank earns interest income from loans (revenue), pays interest on deposits (COGS), and the difference is net interest income (gross profit). After deducting operating costs (branches, staff, IT), it arrives at operating profit.
2. Margins: The True Test of Profitability

Absolute profit figures matter, but margins tell you how efficiently a company converts revenue into profit:

    Gross Margin = (Gross Profit / Revenue) × 100. A high gross margin indicates strong pricing power or low direct costs.

    Operating Margin = (Operating Profit / Revenue) × 100. This shows how well the company controls its overhead.

    Net Margin = (Net Profit / Revenue) × 100. The final profit per shilling of sales.

Compare margins across competitors and over time. A declining margin may signal rising costs, competitive pressure, or poor management. A stable or expanding margin is a positive sign.
3. Real-World Application on the DSE

When you read a company’s annual report, focus on these questions:

    Is revenue growing? If not, why?

    Are costs growing faster than revenue? If so, margins will shrink.

    Is net profit sustainable, or is it boosted by one‑off events (asset sales, tax credits)?

The Daily DSE Wrap often mentions earnings results when they are released. For example, if NMB reports a strong quarter, we might note that its net interest margin improved due to lower funding costs. That’s the income statement at work.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When we cover a stock that has just published results, we will break down the income statement highlights in plain language. Use this article as a reference to understand terms like “top‑line growth,” “margin expansion,” and “earnings beat.”

    Action step: Find the most recent annual report of a DSE company you follow. Locate the income statement and calculate the gross and net margins. Compare them to the previous year. What changed?

Article 2.3 – How to Read a Company’s Financial Statements (Part 2 – The Balance Sheet)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

If the income statement shows how much profit was generated, the balance sheet shows what the company owns and owes at a single point in time. It’s the financial health report — and for an investor, it can reveal hidden strengths or looming dangers that the income statement alone misses.
1. The Fundamental Equation

Every balance sheet follows this equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders’ Equity

    Assets are what the company owns: cash, buildings, machinery, inventory, and amounts owed by customers.

    Liabilities are what it owes: loans, supplier payments, and bonds issued.

    Shareholders’ Equity is what’s left for the owners after all debts are paid. It’s the net worth of the company.

2. Key Balance Sheet Items and What They Reveal
Item	Why It Matters
Cash and equivalents	Liquidity buffer. Can the company survive a shock?
Accounts receivable	Money owed by customers. A fast‑growing receivables balance may signal collection problems.
Inventory	For manufacturers like TCC and TPCC. Too much inventory ties up cash; too little may mean lost sales.
Property, plant & equipment (PPE)	Fixed assets. Heavy investment may signal expansion, but also high depreciation costs.
Short‑term debt	Debt due within a year. Must be manageable relative to cash and operating cash flow.
Long‑term debt	Longer repayment. Excessive debt can choke a company in a downturn.
Retained earnings	Cumulative profits not paid as dividends. Show how much the company has reinvested.
3. The Debt‑to‑Equity Ratio: A Vital Check

This ratio = Total Liabilities / Shareholders’ Equity.

It measures how much debt a company uses to fund its assets. A ratio above 2.0 is generally considered high for non‑financial companies, though banks naturally have higher leverage. A company with a debt‑to‑equity ratio of 0.5 uses 50 cents of debt for every shilling of equity — relatively conservative. One with 3.0 is aggressively leveraged and may struggle if interest rates rise or earnings fall.

When we analyse a banking stock in the Daily DSE Wrap, we might mention its loan‑to‑deposit ratio, which is conceptually similar: it tells you how much the bank has lent relative to its deposit base.
4. Working Capital: The Short‑Term Health Check

Working capital = Current Assets – Current Liabilities.

Positive working capital means the company can meet its short‑term obligations. Negative working capital may indicate trouble — or, in some industries (like retailers), it can be normal. Check the trend: worsening working capital may signal that the company is struggling to collect from customers or is stocking too much inventory.
5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In our market analysis, we rarely quote balance sheet figures directly. But the health of the balance sheet determines whether a company can pay dividends, invest in growth, or survive a recession. When we say “CRDB has a strong capital base,” that’s a balance sheet statement. When we note that a company has issued a large bond, we are observing a liability increase.

    Action step: Pick a DSE company. Find its balance sheet in the annual report. Calculate the debt‑to‑equity ratio. Is it comfortably below 1.0, or is it stretched?

Article 2.4 – The Cash Flow Statement: Where the Money Really Is

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. A company can report a healthy net profit on its income statement but still go bankrupt if it runs out of cash. The cash flow statement shows the actual movement of money in and out of the business. For a DSE investor, it’s the ultimate truth‑test.
1. The Three Sections of the Cash Flow Statement

Every cash flow statement is divided into three parts:

    Operating Activities: Cash generated from the core business — selling goods or services, paying salaries, collecting from customers. This is the most important section. Positive operating cash flow is the sign of a healthy, self‑sustaining business.

    Investing Activities: Cash spent on buying assets (new factories, equipment) or received from selling them. Negative investing cash flow is not necessarily bad — it may mean the company is expanding. But it must be funded by operating cash flow or prudent borrowing.

    Financing Activities: Cash raised from issuing shares or taking loans, and cash paid out as dividends or debt repayments. This section shows how the company funds its operations and growth.

2. Free Cash Flow: The Investor’s Key Metric

Free Cash Flow (FCF) = Operating Cash Flow – Capital Expenditures (CapEx).

FCF is the cash a company has left after maintaining or expanding its asset base. This cash can be used to pay dividends, buy back shares, reduce debt, or reinvest for further growth. Companies that consistently generate strong free cash flow are highly prized by investors.

When you read about a company’s results, look for FCF. A company that reports rising profits but declining or negative FCF may be using aggressive accounting to inflate earnings. Cash flow does not lie.
3. Real DSE Application

Consider a cement company. It might report strong operating cash flow because it sells cement at a profit. But if it is building a new plant, its investing cash flow will be heavily negative — it’s spending cash on expansion. That’s acceptable as long as the expansion is likely to generate future returns. However, if the company is borrowing heavily to fund that expansion and operating cash flow is weak, it’s a red flag.

In our Daily DSE Wrap, when we mention a company’s capital expenditure plans or dividend declaration, we are indirectly referencing the cash flow statement. The ability to pay a growing dividend almost always requires strong free cash flow.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In the Daily Wrap, we regularly report dividend announcements and corporate actions. Those are direct outcomes of cash flow decisions. When TBL declares a TZS 818 per share dividend, the company is signalling that it generated enough cash to reward shareholders. When NMB retains earnings to fund regional expansion, that’s a financing decision visible in the cash flow statement.

    Action step: For any DSE stock you hold, look up its latest cash flow statement. Calculate free cash flow. Is it positive? Is it growing? Compare it to net profit. If FCF is far below net profit, investigate why.

Article 2.5 – Competitive Moats: Why Some Companies Stay on Top

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Why does one bank dominate lending year after year? Why can a brewery raise prices without losing customers? The answer is a competitive moat — a durable advantage that protects a company from rivals, just as a medieval moat protected a castle. Understanding moats is central to long‑term investing success.
1. Types of Competitive Moats
Moat Type	What It Means	DSE Example
Brand	Consumers trust and prefer the brand, paying a premium.	TBL’s Safari Lager, Kilimanjaro Beer — strong local loyalty.
Cost advantage	The company can produce at a lower cost than competitors, either through scale, superior technology, or access to cheap raw materials.	TPCC’s local limestone reserves give it a cost edge in cement.
Network effect	The service becomes more valuable as more people use it.	VODA’s mobile money network: every new user makes the platform more useful for all.
Regulatory licence	The government limits competition through licensing.	Banks need a banking licence from the BOT, creating a barrier to entry.
High switching costs	It is expensive or inconvenient for customers to leave.	A corporate client with multiple loan facilities, payroll services, and a relationship manager at CRDB is unlikely to switch to another bank easily.
2. How to Identify a Moat

Ask these questions:

    Can the company raise prices without losing significant sales? If yes, it has pricing power — a strong sign of a moat.

    Does it consistently earn higher returns on equity (ROE) than its peers? A moated company should be more profitable over time.

    Has its market share been stable or growing? A wide moat helps defend market share.

    Would a new competitor struggle to replicate what this company does? If the answer is yes, the moat is real.

3. Moats and the DSE: What to Watch

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we often comment on sector dynamics that hint at moat strength or weakness. When a company like NMB reports higher loan growth than its peers, it might be leveraging its branch network (a moat). When TBL’s volumes hold up despite a price increase, brand loyalty is at work.

A moat is not permanent. New technology (mobile banking disrupting physical branches), regulatory changes, or poor management can erode a moat. That’s why we track industry trends closely.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When we discuss a company’s competitive position in the Daily Wrap, we are evaluating its moat. For example, if a new entrant starts taking market share from an established player, we would flag it as a potential moat erosion. Conversely, if a company announces a major capital investment that will widen its cost advantage, we would note the strengthening moat.

    Action step: Pick your favourite DSE stock. Write down what you believe its competitive moat is. Then test that belief: could a competitor with enough money replicate it? If the answer is yes, the moat may be narrower than you think.

Article 2.6 – How Management Decisions Create (or Destroy) Value

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Even a great company with a wide moat can be ruined by poor management. Conversely, a mediocre business can be transformed by an exceptional leadership team. As investors, we must learn to evaluate the people making the decisions. This article gives you a practical framework.
1. The Five Key Management Decisions
Decision Area	What Good Management Does	Warning Signs
Capital allocation	Invests in high‑return projects; returns excess cash to shareholders via dividends or buybacks.	Chases growth for its own sake; makes expensive acquisitions that destroy value.
Capital structure	Maintains a prudent level of debt; matches funding to asset life.	Loads the company with excessive debt, especially short‑term.
Dividend policy	Pays sustainable dividends that grow over time, retaining enough for reinvestment.	Cuts dividends suddenly without a clear strategic reason; borrows to pay dividends.
Transparency	Communicates openly about strategy, risks, and performance.	Uses complex language, hides losses, changes accounting policies frequently.
Culture & Governance	Fosters an ethical, long‑oriented culture; board is independent and experienced.	Conflicts of interest; family members placed in key roles without merit; weak board oversight.
2. How to Assess Management on the DSE

You cannot sit in the boardroom, but you can observe:

    Read the chairman’s and CEO’s letters in the annual report. Are they honest about challenges? Do they set clear goals and report back on progress?

    Compare return on equity (ROE) over time. A consistent ROE suggests disciplined capital allocation.

    Check insider trading announcements. Heavy selling by directors without a clear reason (like diversification) can be a red flag.

    Review the dividend history. A stable or growing dividend signals confidence in future cash flows.

In our Daily DSE Wrap, we sometimes note when a company’s management announces a new strategy or a major investment. We analyse the decision through this lens: will this strengthen or weaken shareholder value?
3. The Dangers of Empire Building

A common management trap is “empire building” — pursuing growth for the sake of size, not profitability. A bank that opens branches in areas with low demand just to boast the largest network is not building value. A manufacturer that acquires a struggling competitor at a premium is likely destroying it. The Daily Wrap may highlight such moves with a cautious tone.

Good management thinks like an owner. When you invest, you become a part‑owner. You want your management to treat your capital as carefully as they would treat their own.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When a DSE company announces a major decision — a dividend increase, a share buyback, an acquisition, a change in CEO — we cover it in the Daily Wrap with an analysis of what it means for long‑term investors. Use this article as a checklist to evaluate those announcements yourself.

    Action step: Find the most recent CEO’s letter from a DSE company you own. Does it clearly state what the company achieved, where it fell short, and what the plan is for the year ahead? If it reads like a brochure and avoids hard topics, be wary.

Article 3.1 – What Is a Stock Exchange? Inside the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

We now shift from analysing the economy and companies to the arena where they meet: the stock exchange. The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange (DSE) is the physical and electronic marketplace where ownership of Tanzanian companies is bought and sold. This article is your guided tour.
1. The Purpose of a Stock Exchange

A stock exchange serves three vital functions:

    Capital raising: Companies sell shares to the public (IPO, rights issue) to raise funds for expansion.

    Liquidity: Investors can buy and sell shares easily, converting their ownership into cash.

    Price discovery: Through the interaction of thousands of buyers and sellers, the market determines a fair price for each company at every moment.

The DSE operates under the authority of the Capital Markets and Securities Authority (CMSA), which regulates participants and protects investors.
2. The DSE: A Brief History and Structure

The DSE was incorporated in 1996 and trading began in 1998. It is located in Dar es Salaam and has grown from a handful of listed companies to over 20, including cross‑listings from Kenya (KCB, NMG) and others. Trading days are Monday to Friday, excluding public holidays, from 10:00 am to 3:00 pm East Africa Time.

The DSE uses an automated trading system. Investors place orders through licensed stockbrokers, and trades are settled through the Central Securities Depository (CSD). The CSD holds your shares electronically — there are no paper share certificates.
3. The DSE Indices: Your Market Thermometer

The main benchmarks are:

    DSE All Share Index (DSEI): Tracks all listed companies. When we say “the market rose,” we mean the DSEI went up.

    Tanzania Share Index (TSI): Excludes cross‑listed companies like KCB and NMG, focusing on purely Tanzanian firms.

    Sector indices: Banks, Finance & Investment (BI); Industrial & Allied (IA); Commercial Services (CS). Each tracks a specific part of the market.

In every Daily DSE Wrap, we report these indices so you can see at a glance which segments of the market are moving.
4. Who Are the Market Participants?

    Retail investors: Individual Tanzanians investing their savings.

    Institutional investors: Pension funds (NSSF, PPF), insurance companies, and collective investment schemes (UTT AMIS).

    Foreign investors: International funds and diaspora investors buying into frontier markets.

    Market makers: (not yet fully implemented on the DSE) but certain brokers provide continuous buy and sell quotes to improve liquidity.

The Daily Wrap’s foreign flow data tells you what percentage of the day’s turnover came from foreign buyers and sellers. This is a critical barometer of international sentiment toward Tanzania.
5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Market Pulse section of every Daily Wrap is essentially a daily health check on the DSE. The indices, turnover, volume, deals, and foreign participation all come directly from the exchange’s end‑of‑day report. By understanding what the DSE is and how it works, you can interpret that data with confidence.

    Action step: On the DSE website, look up the list of licensed stockbrokers. Familiarise yourself with the names. One day you will open an account with one of them.

Article 3.2 – Stocks, Bonds, and ETFs: The Three Building Blocks

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

On the DSE, you don’t just buy “investments” — you buy specific types of securities, each with its own risk, return, and purpose. The three most common are stocks, bonds, and ETFs. This article explains what each is and when you might use them.
1. Stocks (Equities): Ownership in a Business

When you buy a share of CRDB or TBL, you become a part‑owner. You share in the profits (through dividends and capital appreciation) and the risks (if the company fails, your shares can become worthless). Stocks offer the highest long‑term return potential among traditional assets but are also the most volatile.

Key features:

    Dividends: cash paid from profits.

    Capital gains: increase in share price.

    Voting rights: in theory, though small shareholders rarely influence decisions.

2. Bonds: Lending to the Government or a Company

A bond is a loan. When you buy a Tanzanian government bond, you are lending money to the government in exchange for a fixed interest payment (coupon) and the return of your principal at maturity. Corporate bonds work the same way but are issued by companies.

Bonds are generally safer than stocks but offer lower returns. They are suitable for conservative investors or as a stabiliser in a portfolio. The DSE bond market is active, with government bonds dominating.

Key features:

    Fixed coupon payments (e.g., 15.95% per year on one recent 25‑year bond).

    Face value repaid at maturity.

    Price fluctuates before maturity based on interest rate changes.

In the Daily Wrap, we report bond turnover and yields. When bond trading surges, it often signals that large investors are shifting money out of equities and into fixed income — an important rotation signal.
3. ETFs (Exchange‑Traded Funds): A Basket of Securities

An ETF holds a basket of stocks or bonds and trades on the exchange like a single stock. The DSE lists two ETFs: the IEACLC-ETF and VERTEX-ETF. Buying an ETF gives you instant diversification across multiple underlying assets.

ETFs are ideal for beginners who want market exposure without picking individual stocks. They typically charge a small annual fee (expense ratio). In the 29 June 2026 report, IEACLC-ETF traded 354,823 shares at TZS 1,250, and VERTEX-ETF traded 104,839 shares at TZS 335.
4. Building a Portfolio with These Blocks

A balanced investor might hold:

    60% equities for growth (stocks, ETFs).

    30% bonds for income and stability.

    10% cash for liquidity and opportunities.

The exact mix depends on your age, goals, and risk tolerance. We cover portfolio construction fully in Module 5.
5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In every Daily Wrap, we report the equity turnover, the bond turnover, and the ETF activity — all in one place. This gives you a complete picture of where money is flowing. Use this article to understand what those numbers represent.

Article 3.3 – How a Trade Happens: From Your Phone to the Market

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You decide to buy 100 shares of CRDB. You call your broker or use a mobile app. What happens next? Understanding the mechanics of a trade removes the mystery and helps you avoid costly mistakes. This article traces the journey of an order from start to settlement.
1. Placing the Order

You contact a licensed DSE stockbroker (or an authorised agent) and specify:

    Ticker: The unique code for the company (e.g., CRDB).

    Quantity: Number of shares.

    Price limit: The maximum you will pay (if buying) or the minimum you will accept (if selling). You can also place a “market order,” which executes at the best available price immediately, but limit orders offer more control.

Your broker enters the order into the DSE’s Automated Trading System (ATS).
2. The Order Book: Where Supply Meets Demand

The ATS maintains an electronic order book — a list of all outstanding buy (bid) and sell (ask/offer) orders, sorted by price and time. The best bid is the highest price any buyer is willing to pay. The best ask is the lowest price any seller is willing to accept. The difference between them is the spread.

A trade occurs when a buy order matches a sell order — either a buyer accepts the seller’s asking price, or a seller lowers their price to meet a bid.

In the DSE daily price tables, you see columns for “Outstanding Bids” and “Outstanding Offers.” These show the depth of demand and supply at the close of trading.
3. Clearing and Settlement: You Become the Owner

Once a trade executes, the DSE sends the details to the Central Securities Depository (CSD). The CSD updates ownership records: shares move from the seller’s account to yours, and cash moves from your broker’s settlement account to the seller’s broker. This process takes T+2 (trade date plus two business days) for equities on the DSE.

Your shares are held electronically in your CSD account. You receive a statement confirming the transaction, but you don’t receive a paper certificate.
4. Why This Matters to You

Understanding the mechanics helps you:

    Set realistic limit orders: If the spread is wide (e.g., bid at TZS 2,550, ask at TZS 2,650), a limit order at the midpoint might not fill immediately. You may need to be patient or adjust.

    Interpret volume and deals: High volume and many deals indicate strong liquidity — buyers and sellers are finding each other easily. Low volume with wide spreads suggests caution.

    Avoid errors: Always confirm the ticker, quantity, and price before finalising an order.

5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In the Daily Wrap, we report the number of deals and total volume. A high deal count with moderate volume often means many small retail orders, while a few large deals with massive volume may signal institutional activity. The outstanding bids and offers for key stocks, when mentioned, reveal the supply‑demand balance.

Article 3.4 – Understanding Market Data: Turnover, Volume, and Deals

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Every trading day, the DSE publishes numbers that look like this: “Turnover: TZS 7,088.98 million, Volume: 1,734,477 shares, Deals: 4,704.” These figures appear in our Daily DSE Wrap and in our Current Prices table. They are not just statistics — they are the market’s voice. This article teaches you to listen.
1. Turnover: The Money Flow

Turnover is the total value of shares traded in a session. It’s the most important liquidity indicator. A rising turnover trend over several weeks suggests growing investor interest and confidence. A sudden spike in turnover without a corresponding price move may indicate a large institutional trade (block trade) or an accumulation/distribution phase.

In the 29 June 2026 market, equity turnover was TZS 7.09 billion. This was down sharply from the previous session’s record, but still well above the TZS 3 billion that marks a quiet day. The high turnover tells us the market is liquid and that meaningful money is flowing.
2. Volume: The Quantity of Shares Traded

Volume is the total number of shares that changed hands. Compare volume to the number of outstanding shares to gauge how active a stock is. A high volume on a stock like CRDB (928,067 shares on 29 June) indicates deep liquidity — you can buy or sell a large block without moving the price dramatically. A low volume on a smaller counter means even a modest order could push the price.

Volume can confirm a trend. A rising price accompanied by expanding volume is a strong signal of genuine demand. A rising price on declining volume may be fragile.
3. Deals: The Participation Count

The number of deals tells you how many separate buy‑sell transactions occurred. A high deal count relative to volume suggests broad retail participation — many investors making small trades. A low deal count with very high volume suggests large block trades between institutions.

On 29 June, 4,704 deals were recorded despite volume being moderate. That indicates a lot of small retail and local institutional orders — precisely the kind of broad‑based participation that signals a healthy, domestically driven market.
4. Putting It All Together

Consider this scenario: Turnover surges, volume jumps, deals spike, and the DSEI rises. Interpretation: strong, broad‑based buying with institutional and retail participation. The rally is likely sustainable.

Now imagine: Turnover rises, but volume and deals fall, and the DSEI barely moves. That might be a single large cross‑trade (block) that doesn’t reflect genuine market direction.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we combine these three metrics — turnover, volume, deals — with the index movements to tell the day’s story. When you read, “Deal count surged to 4,704, signalling broad retail and local institutional participation,” you now know exactly what that means.

Article 3.5 – The Difference Between Primary and Secondary Markets

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

When you buy shares of CRDB through your broker on a Tuesday afternoon, you are participating in the secondary market — the day‑to‑day exchange of already‑issued securities. But CRDB originally sold its shares to the public in an IPO (initial public offering) in the primary market. Understanding the distinction is essential because each market serves a different purpose and offers different opportunities.
1. The Primary Market: Companies Raise Fresh Capital

In the primary market, a company or government issues new securities and sells them directly to investors. The proceeds go to the issuer. This happens through:

    IPO (Initial Public Offering): A company lists on the DSE for the first time.

    Rights issue: Existing shareholders are given the right to buy additional shares at a discount, in proportion to their current holding. This is a way to raise capital from those already invested.

    Bond auction: The government or a corporation issues new bonds, and investors bid for them.

Primary market offerings are often covered in the Daily DSE Wrap when they are announced, because they can affect existing shareholders (dilution in a rights issue) and signal management’s growth plans.
2. The Secondary Market: Investors Trade Among Themselves

The secondary market is the DSE’s daily trading activity — all the buying and selling you see in the wrap. Money changes hands between investors; the company does not receive any new capital. The secondary market provides liquidity, allowing you to exit an investment or build a position at a fair, transparent price.

The DSE All Share Index (DSEI) reflects secondary market prices. When the index rises, the value of existing holdings goes up, even though no new capital flowed to the companies.
3. Why the Distinction Matters

    Valuation context: Shares bought in an IPO are often priced at a discount to encourage participation. After listing, secondary market trading determines the real price. A share that “pops” on listing may have been undervalued in the IPO.

    Dilution awareness: A rights issue in the primary market increases the number of shares outstanding. If you don’t participate, your ownership percentage shrinks. You must decide whether to invest more to maintain your stake.

    Bond strategies: Buying a government bond at auction (primary) and holding to maturity avoids secondary market price fluctuations. Buying in the secondary market allows you to sell before maturity but exposes you to interest rate risk.

4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In the Daily Wrap, we report the secondary market data every day. When primary market events occur — an announced IPO, a rights issue, a bond auction — we provide dedicated analysis. Understanding the distinction helps you interpret that analysis correctly.

    Action step: Think about your own portfolio. Did you acquire your shares in the secondary market? If your company announces a rights issue, will you participate, or will you accept dilution? Knowing the difference now prepares you for that decision.

Article 4.1 – What Is Fundamental Analysis? The Art of Finding True Value

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You now understand the economy, companies, and the marketplace. It’s time to put it all together. Fundamental analysis is the process of evaluating a company’s intrinsic value by examining its financial statements, competitive position, industry, and the broader economy. If the intrinsic value is significantly higher than the current market price, the stock may be undervalued — a buying opportunity. If the market price is far above intrinsic value, it may be overvalued — a reason to sell or avoid.
1. The Core Principle: Price vs. Value

The father of fundamental analysis, Benjamin Graham, taught that in the short run the market is a voting machine (popularity), but in the long run it is a weighing machine (substance). Your job is to estimate the substance — the true worth of the business — and compare it to the current market price.

Intrinsic value is not a single number. It’s a range, based on reasonable assumptions about future cash flows, growth, and risk. Different analysts will arrive at different estimates, but the discipline of trying to calculate it keeps you from overpaying.
2. The Two Main Approaches

    Valuation multiples (relative valuation): Compare the company’s price to its earnings, book value, or sales, and then compare those multiples to peers or the company’s own history. We cover this in Article 4.2.

    Discounted cash flow (DCF) (absolute valuation): Project the company’s future free cash flows and discount them back to today’s shillings using an appropriate discount rate. This is more complex but forces you to think deeply about the business.

For most DSE investors, relative valuation using simple multiples is a practical starting point.
3. The Fundamental Analysis Checklist

When we analyse a stock in the Daily DSE Wrap, we implicitly use this framework:

    Understand the business: What does the company do? How does it make money?

    Assess the industry: Is the sector growing? What’s the competitive landscape?

    Examine the financials: Revenue and profit growth, margins, debt levels, cash flow.

    Evaluate management: Are they competent and shareholder‑friendly?

    Calculate valuation: Is the stock cheap or expensive relative to its history and peers?

    Consider macro risks: Interest rates, exchange rates, regulation.

4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When the Daily Wrap focuses on a particular stock — as we did with NMB on 29 June — we are applying fundamental analysis in real time. We look at the block trade, the price action, the dividend catalyst, and the sector context to assess whether the stock’s current price makes sense for a long‑horizon allocator.

    Action step: Choose one DSE stock. Use the checklist above to write a one‑page investment thesis. Is it undervalued or overvalued? Why?

Article 4.2 – Key Valuation Ratios Made Simple (P/E, P/B, Dividend Yield)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Ratios are the shorthand of fundamental analysis. They allow you to compare companies of different sizes and to assess whether a stock is cheap or expensive at a glance. This article explains the three most widely used ratios on the DSE, with concrete examples.
1. Price‑to‑Earnings Ratio (P/E)

Formula: P/E = Share Price / Earnings per Share (EPS)

The P/E tells you how many years of current earnings it would take to recoup the purchase price, assuming earnings remain constant. A high P/E suggests investors expect strong future growth; a low P/E may signal a bargain — or a company in trouble.

On the DSE, banks often trade at P/E ratios between 5 and 12, while consumer companies may command higher multiples due to more predictable earnings. For example, if CRDB earns TZS 250 per share and trades at TZS 2,590, its P/E is about 10.4. That’s reasonable for a stable bank in a growing economy.

Watch for: Extraordinary items that distort earnings. Use “normalised” or “core” EPS if available, or simply look at the trend over several years.
2. Price‑to‑Book Ratio (P/B)

Formula: P/B = Share Price / Book Value per Share

Book value is the company’s assets minus liabilities — essentially its net worth per share. A P/B below 1.0 means the market values the company at less than its accounting net worth, which might indicate a bargain or a company that is destroying value. Banks are often analysed using P/B because their assets (loans) and liabilities (deposits) are marked to market or relatively transparent.

If NMB trades at TZS 16,160 and has a book value per share of, say, TZS 10,000, its P/B is 1.62. That premium suggests the market expects NMB to generate returns well above its book value.
3. Dividend Yield

Formula: Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Share Price × 100%

This tells you the cash return you receive just for holding the stock. In an environment where bank savings accounts offer 2–3%, a 5%+ dividend yield from a stable company is highly attractive.

On 29 June, TBL traded at TZS 9,710 and paid an annual dividend of TZS 818, giving a yield of 8.4%. That’s one reason TBL is widely held by income‑focused investors. Remember: dividends are not guaranteed. A high yield can be a trap if the company cannot sustain the payout.
4. Using Ratios Wisely

No single ratio should decide your investment. Compare a company’s ratios to:

    Its own 5‑year history (is it becoming more or less expensive?).

    Its industry peers (is it undervalued relative to competitors?).

    The overall market (is the DSE cheap or expensive right now?).

In the Daily DSE Wrap, we occasionally reference valuation context — like “the banking sector trades at a discount to regional peers on a price‑to‑book basis.” That’s a macro observation built from these micro ratios.

    Action step: For three DSE stocks you follow, look up their current P/E, P/B, and dividend yield. Write them down. Compare them to a regional peer (e.g., KCB on the NSE). What do the numbers suggest about relative value?

Article 4.3 – How to Analyse a Sector: Banking

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Banks are the backbone of the DSE, accounting for a significant portion of total market capitalisation and daily turnover. CRDB, NMB, KCB, DCB, MKCB, MCB, and MUCOBA are among the most actively traded counters. Analysing a bank is different from analysing a manufacturer because a bank’s raw material is money itself. This article gives you a specialised toolkit.
1. The Banking Business Model

A bank takes deposits (pays interest to savers) and lends that money to borrowers (charges a higher rate). The difference — the net interest margin (NIM) — is its core profit. Banks also earn fee income from transaction services, foreign exchange, and mobile banking.

Key revenue drivers:

    Loan book growth: More lending, more interest income.

    Fee income: Non‑interest revenue from services.

    Cost control: Efficient operations (low cost‑to‑income ratio).

Key risks:

    Credit risk: Borrowers default. Watch the non‑performing loan (NPL) ratio.

    Interest rate risk: If deposit rates rise faster than lending rates, margins compress.

    Liquidity risk: A sudden withdrawal of deposits can strain the bank.

2. The Metrics That Matter
Metric	What It Tells You	Healthy Range
Net Interest Margin (NIM)	The spread between lending and deposit rates.	5–8% is typical for Tanzanian banks
Return on Equity (ROE)	How profitably the bank uses shareholders’ capital.	Above 15% is good; above 20% is excellent
Return on Assets (ROA)	Profit generated per shilling of assets.	Above 1.5% is good
Cost‑to‑Income Ratio	How much it costs to generate a shilling of revenue.	Below 50% is efficient
NPL Ratio	Percentage of loans that are not being repaid.	Below 5% is comfortable
Loan‑to‑Deposit Ratio	How much of the deposit base is lent out.	70–90% balances profitability and liquidity
Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)	The bank’s cushion against losses, as required by BOT.	Above the regulatory minimum (12%+)

When we discuss a banking stock in the Daily DSE Wrap, we often reference these metrics. For example, if NMB reports a strong quarter, we might highlight its ROE or its improving NPL ratio.
3. How to Analyse a Bank Step‑by‑Step

    Read the annual report, focusing on the management discussion of the loan book, deposits, and strategy.

    Calculate NIM, ROE, ROA, and the cost‑to‑income ratio for the last three years. Look for trends.

    Examine the NPL ratio. Is it rising? If so, which sectors (agriculture, SMEs, personal) are struggling?

    Check the capital adequacy ratio. A bank with a CAR well above the minimum has more room to grow its loan book.

    Assess the competitive position. Does the bank have a wide branch network? A strong mobile platform? Loyal corporate clients?

    Consider the macro environment. Are interest rates rising or falling? Is the economy growing? These directly affect bank profitability.

4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Banking, Finance & Investment (BI) index is reported in every Daily Wrap. When you see it rise, it often reflects positive sentiment about loan growth and economic expansion. When you see a surge in a specific bank’s volume — like NMB’s block trade on 29 June — it signals institutional positioning, which you can now analyse using the metrics above.

    Action step: Pick one DSE bank. Download its most recent annual report. Calculate its NIM, ROE, and NPL ratio. Compare to another DSE bank. Which is stronger?

Article 4.4 – How to Analyse a Sector: Industrials & Consumer Goods

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Tanzania’s industrial and consumer goods sector includes some of the DSE’s most recognisable names: TBL, TCC, TPCC, TOL, and TCCL (cigarette manufacturer). These companies make things — beer, cement, oil products — and their fortunes are tied to the physical economy. This article explains how to analyse them.
1. The Industrial Business Model

Industrial companies take raw materials (limestone for cement, barley and hops for beer, crude oil for fuel) and transform them into finished products. They earn profits by selling at a price above the cost of production. Key drivers:

    Capacity utilisation: How much of the factory’s potential output is actually being produced. Higher utilisation spreads fixed costs over more units.

    Input costs: Raw materials, energy, and transport are major expenses. A spike in global oil prices, for example, squeezes TOL’s margins.

    Pricing power: The ability to pass cost increases to customers. TBL’s brand strength gives it significant pricing power.

    Demand: Driven by population growth, urbanisation, and economic expansion. More construction means more cement demand (TCC, TPCC). More celebrations mean more beer (TBL).

2. Key Metrics for Industrials
Metric	Why It Matters
Revenue growth	Are sales volumes rising, or is growth only from price increases?
Gross margin	Reveals pricing power and cost control. A stable or rising gross margin is positive.
Operating margin	Reflects efficiency in running factories and distribution.
Return on capital employed (ROCE)	Measures how well the company uses its capital (factories, machinery) to generate profit. Higher is better.
Debt‑to‑equity	Heavy industry often requires large debt to build plants. Ensure the debt load is serviceable.
Capacity expansion plans	New factories or production lines signal future growth but involve execution risk.
3. Consumer Goods Specifics

Consumer goods companies — brewers, cigarette makers, food processors — depend on brand loyalty, distribution reach, and volume. Key additional factors:

    Market share: Is the company gaining or losing share?

    Product mix: Are consumers moving to premium products (higher margin) or value lines?

    Excise tax sensitivity: Brewing and cigarette companies are heavily taxed. A budget change can significantly impact earnings.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, when we mention that TBL volumes held up despite a price increase, we are observing pricing power in action. When we note that the government has raised excise duties, we are flagging a potential earnings headwind.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Industrial & Allied (IA) index is reported daily. Movements in IA often reflect news about infrastructure spending, commodity prices, or budget tax changes. When you see the IA index move, you can now diagnose which sub‑factors are at play.

Article 4.5 – Reading the Story Behind the Numbers: Annual Report Analysis

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

The annual report is the single most important document a public company produces. It contains the financial statements, but also the management’s narrative, the auditor’s opinion, and detailed notes. Learning to extract the story from a report separates a savvy investor from a casual speculator.
1. Where to Start

Open the annual report and begin with these sections, in this order:

    Chairman’s / CEO’s letter: Read this first. It sets the tone. Does management acknowledge challenges or only boast? Look for specific goals and whether previous goals were met.

    Auditor’s report: Look for the words “unqualified opinion” — that means the auditor found the financials to be fairly presented. Any “qualified” opinion or “emphasis of matter” is a red flag.

    Financial statements: Income statement, balance sheet, cash flow statement — you now know what each reveals.

    Notes to the financial statements: Often the most revealing part. They explain accounting policies, breakdown debt, detail contingent liabilities, and disclose related‑party transactions.

2. Red Flags in an Annual Report

    Frequent changes in accounting policies: May be an attempt to hide poor performance.

    Large related‑party transactions: Loans or deals with directors’ other companies that are not at arm’s length.

    Rising receivables that outpace revenue growth: Cash may not be coming in as fast as it should.

    Overly complex language: A report designed to confuse rather than inform.

    Dividend cut without explanation: Signals cash flow problems or a shift in priorities.

3. The DSE Context

Listed companies in Tanzania are required to publish audited annual reports within a few months of their financial year‑end. Most DSE companies have December year‑ends, so reports arrive between March and May. The Daily DSE Wrap will often highlight key results and compare them to expectations.

When we say “CRDB reported a 12% increase in net profit, driven by loan book expansion,” that conclusion came directly from an analysis of the income statement and management’s commentary.
