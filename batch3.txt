Article 4.6 – An Introduction to Technical Analysis (It’s Not Sorcery)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

While fundamental analysis tells you what to buy, technical analysis attempts to tell you when to buy or sell by studying price charts, volume, and patterns. It’s not a crystal ball, but a supplementary tool that many institutional investors use to refine their entry and exit points. This article introduces the most practical concepts for a DSE investor.
1. The Foundation: Price and Trends

A trend is the general direction in which a price is moving. An uptrend consists of higher highs and higher lows; a downtrend has lower lows and lower highs. The simplest technical rule is: Don’t fight the trend.

    A stock in a clear uptrend with rising volume is under accumulation.

    A stock in a downtrend with high volume may be under distribution (selling).

On the DSE, daily price charts are available from various data services, though not yet as seamlessly as on developed markets. You can manually plot closing prices to observe trends.
2. Support and Resistance

    Support is a price level where buying interest is strong enough to prevent further decline. Think of it as a floor.

    Resistance is a price level where selling pressure halts a rally. Think of it as a ceiling.

These levels often form at previous highs or lows. For example, if CRDB consistently finds buyers at TZS 2,550, that level becomes support. If it repeatedly fails to break above TZS 2,600, that’s resistance.

In the Daily DSE Wrap, when we note that a stock “bounced off support” or “tested resistance,” we are using these concepts.
3. Volume as a Confirmation Tool

Volume confirms price moves. A breakout above resistance on significantly higher volume is more likely to be genuine. A breakout on low volume may be a false signal. Conversely, if a stock falls below support on heavy volume, it suggests real selling pressure.
4. Simple Moving Averages (SMA)

A 50‑day simple moving average is the average closing price over the last 50 trading days. It smooths out noise and shows the medium‑term trend. When the current price is above the 50‑day SMA, the trend is considered positive; below, negative. A “golden cross” (50‑day SMA crossing above 200‑day SMA) is a classic long‑term bullish signal, though it works best in liquid markets.
5. Limitations on the DSE

Technical analysis works best in highly liquid, continuous markets. The DSE’s lower liquidity on some counters means that a few large orders can create misleading chart patterns. Use technical analysis as a supplement to, never a replacement for, fundamental analysis. In our Daily DSE Wrap, we primarily rely on fundamentals but will occasionally note a technically significant level when it aligns with our fundamental view.

    Action step: For your favourite DSE stock, manually note the closing prices over the last 10 trading days. Draw a line connecting them. Is there a visible trend? Where might support and resistance lie?

Article 5.1 – Risk and Return: The Only Free Lunch Is Diversification

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Every investment carries risk. The key is not to avoid risk — that’s impossible — but to understand it, manage it, and ensure you are compensated for bearing it. This article explains the relationship between risk and return and introduces the most powerful risk‑management tool available to every investor: diversification.
1. Types of Investment Risk
Risk Type	What It Means	Can It Be Diversified Away?
Company‑specific risk	A CEO resigns, a factory burns down, a product fails.	Yes — hold many companies, and no single failure will sink you.
Sector risk	A regulatory change hits all banks, or a drought hits all brewers.	Partially — hold companies in different sectors.
Market risk (systematic)	A recession, a currency crisis, a global pandemic.	No — this risk affects all assets. You manage it through asset allocation (bonds, cash, etc.) and a long time horizon.
2. The Risk‑Return Trade‑Off

Higher expected return comes with higher risk. Government bonds offer lower returns but are considered very safe. Equities offer higher returns but can lose significant value in a downturn. As an investor, you must decide where on the risk spectrum you belong, based on your goals, age, and temperament.
3. Diversification: The Free Lunch

Diversification means spreading your money across different companies, sectors, and asset classes so that a loss in one area can be offset by a gain in another. It’s the most reliable way to reduce risk without reducing expected return.

A simple diversified DSE portfolio might hold:

    A major bank (CRDB or NMB)

    A consumer goods company (TBL)

    An industrial (TCC or TPCC)

    A telecom (VODA)

    A bond or bond fund for stability

Owning 4–5 well‑chosen, unrelated companies dramatically reduces company‑specific risk.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

In the Daily Wrap, we track sector performance, which shows you diversification in action. On any given day, one sector may rise while another falls. A diversified portfolio captures the gains without being crushed by the losses. When we note that banks led but industrials lagged, that’s diversification at work.

    Action step: List your current holdings. How many different sectors are represented? Could one event wipe out a large portion of your portfolio? If so, consider diversifying.

Article 5.3 – The Power of Compounding and Why Time Beats Timing

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. For a Tanzanian investor with a long horizon, compounding is the single most powerful force for building wealth. This article shows you why starting early and staying invested matters far more than finding the perfect entry point.
1. What Is Compounding?

Compounding is earning returns on your returns. If you invest TZS 1,000,000 and it grows 10% in one year, you have TZS 1,100,000. If the next year it grows another 10%, you earn TZS 110,000 (not TZS 100,000) because the gain is calculated on the new, higher balance. Over many years, this snowball effect becomes enormous.
2. The Mathematics of Compounding

Suppose you invest TZS 1,000,000 at an average annual return of 10%:
Year	Balance
0	1,000,000
5	1,610,510
10	2,593,742
20	6,727,500
30	17,449,402

Notice that in the last 10 years, the portfolio grew by more than TZS 10 million — even though you added no new money. That’s compounding.

If you reinvest dividends — buying more shares with the cash you receive — the effect accelerates even further.
3. Time in the Market vs. Timing the Market

Many investors try to buy at the bottom and sell at the top. The evidence is clear: almost no one can do this consistently. Missing just the best 10 trading days in a decade can cut your returns in half. The investor who stays fully invested, reinvests dividends, and ignores the noise almost always outperforms the one who jumps in and out.

On the DSE, where some stocks trade infrequently, trying to time is especially risky. A long‑horizon, buy‑and‑hold approach suits the market’s structure well.
4. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Daily Wrap is a tool for staying informed, not for timing the market. It helps you understand what is happening, but it does not advocate trading daily. We publish it because an informed investor is a confident investor, and a confident investor can sit through volatility without panic‑selling.

    Action step: Use a compound interest calculator (many free online) to project your portfolio’s value in 10, 20, and 30 years, assuming a realistic return and your current savings rate. Let the numbers motivate your patience.

Article 5.4 – Behavioural Finance: Understanding Your Own Worst Enemy

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

You can master macroeconomic cycles, financial statements, and valuation techniques, and still fail as an investor if you cannot master your own emotions. Behavioural finance studies the psychological biases that cause investors to make irrational decisions. This article covers the most dangerous biases and how to counter them.
1. Loss Aversion: The Pain of Losing Is Twice as Powerful as the Joy of Winning

People feel the pain of a loss much more intensely than the pleasure of an equivalent gain. This leads to two costly mistakes:

    Holding onto losers too long, hoping they will recover, instead of cutting losses.

    Selling winners too early, to lock in a gain and avoid the pain of seeing it disappear.

The fix: Have a predetermined thesis for every investment. If the thesis is broken, sell regardless of gain or loss. If the thesis is intact, hold through the noise.
2. Herd Mentality: Everyone’s Doing It, So It Must Be Right

When a stock is soaring and everyone is talking about it, the temptation to buy is overwhelming. Conversely, when the market is crashing and panic is everywhere, the urge to sell is almost irresistible. Herd behaviour creates bubbles and crashes.

The fix: Use a checklist (Article 4.7) and a long‑term plan. When you feel the herd’s pull, re‑read your written investment policy.
3. Overconfidence: I Know What Will Happen Next

After a few successful trades, many investors begin to believe they have a special talent for prediction. They trade more frequently, take larger positions, and eventually suffer disproportionate losses.

The fix: Track your decisions. Compare your actual returns to the market index. If you are not beating the DSEI over a full cycle, consider a simpler, more diversified approach.
4. Confirmation Bias: Seeking Information That Supports Your View

You buy a stock, and suddenly you notice only the positive news. You ignore or dismiss negative information. This bias prevents you from objectively reassessing your investments.

The fix: Actively seek out the bear case for every stock you own. Read critical analysis. If you can’t find any, you haven’t looked hard enough.
5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

The Daily Wrap is designed to counter behavioural biases. It presents facts calmly, focuses on the long term, avoids emotional language, and never hypes a stock. Use it as your anchor. When your emotions are screaming “buy!” or “sell!”, re‑read the day’s analysis and ask: What are the facts?

    Action step: Reflect on your last investment decision. Which bias might have been at play? Write it down. Awareness is the first defence.

Article 6.3 – Understanding Tanzanian Tax on Investments

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Taxes can significantly affect your net investment return. Fortunately, Tanzania’s tax regime for listed securities is relatively favourable compared to many countries. This article outlines the main taxes you will encounter as a DSE investor.
1. Withholding Tax on Dividends

Dividends paid by DSE‑listed companies are subject to a 5% final withholding tax for resident individuals. This means that if a company declares a dividend of TZS 1,000 per share, TZS 950 is deposited into your brokerage account, and the remaining TZS 50 goes to the TRA. The tax is “final,” meaning you do not need to declare it further on your personal income tax return.

For non‑resident individuals, the rate may vary based on the double taxation agreement between Tanzania and your country of residence. Common rates range from 5% to 15%.
2. Capital Gains Tax on Listed Securities

As of the current tax code, capital gains from the sale of listed securities are exempt from tax for both residents and non‑residents. This is a major advantage of investing through the DSE. A gain of TZS 1,000,000 is yours entirely.

This exemption is designed to encourage long‑term investment and market participation. However, tax laws can change, so stay informed.
3. Withholding Tax on Bond Interest

Interest earned on government bonds and corporate bonds is subject to a 5% withholding tax for residents. For non‑residents, the rate may be higher under certain conditions.
4. Tax on Collective Investment Schemes

If you invest through a unit trust like UTT AMIS, the tax treatment depends on the underlying assets. Generally, fund income distributed to you is subject to withholding tax at the applicable rate.
5. Record‑Keeping

Keep all contract notes, dividend vouchers, and annual CSD statements. They are your proof of transactions and will be needed if you are ever audited or if you need to reconcile your portfolio.
6. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When we report dividend announcements in the Daily Wrap, we often state the gross dividend and the net (after‑tax) amount. Knowing the tax rate allows you to calculate your true income. For example, the NMB dividend of TZS 610.15 gross yields TZS 579.64 net after the 5% tax.

    Action step: If you hold dividend‑paying stocks, check your contract notes to confirm that withholding tax has been correctly applied. If uncertain, contact your broker.

Article 6.4 – Using Collective Investment Schemes (UTT AMIS, etc.)

By Amana Capital East Africa – Investor Education Series

Not everyone has the time, expertise, or capital to build a diversified portfolio of individual stocks. Collective investment schemes (CIS) — also called unit trusts or mutual funds — offer an alternative. You pool your money with other investors, and a professional fund manager invests it on your behalf. In Tanzania, UTT AMIS is one of the primary providers.
1. How a Unit Trust Works

    You buy units in the fund at the prevailing unit price (Net Asset Value per unit, or NAV).

    The fund invests in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or both.

    Returns come from capital gains on the underlying securities and interest/dividend income.

    You can buy and redeem units on any dealing day.

2. Advantages of Unit Trusts

    Instant diversification even with small amounts.

    Professional management — the fund manager conducts the analysis and trading.

    Liquidity — you can usually redeem units relatively quickly.

    Regulatory oversight — CIS are regulated by CMSA, providing a layer of protection.

3. Types of Funds Available

    Equity funds: Invest primarily in stocks. Higher risk, higher potential return.

    Bond funds: Invest in government and corporate bonds. Lower risk, stable income.

    Balanced funds: Mix of equities and bonds.

    Money market funds: Very low risk, highly liquid, often used as a cash management tool.

4. How to Invest in UTT AMIS or Another CIS

    Visit the fund manager’s office or website.

    Complete an application form and provide KYC documents (ID, TIN, photo).

    Deposit the minimum investment amount (often TZS 100,000 or less).

    Receive a statement showing your unit holdings.

    Monitor the fund’s performance and add to your investment regularly.

5. The Daily DSE Wrap Connection

When we analyse the market, we often mention the bond fund or equity fund activity because these institutional players are significant market participants. Understanding CIS helps you interpret that flow. Additionally, if you are not yet ready to pick individual stocks, a CIS can be a sensible first step while you continue your education.

    Action step: Research two CIS providers in Tanzania. Compare their fees, minimum investments, and historical performance. Consider allocating a small portion of your portfolio to a bond or balanced fund as a diversifier.
