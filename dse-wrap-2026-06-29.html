Banking Sector Leads Volume as Foreign Exit Fades

Daily DSE Wrap | Monday, 29th June 2026

The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange began the week on a positive note, with the All Share Index climbing 14.93 points to close at 4,040.04. While headline equity turnover of TZS 7.09 billion was sharply lower than the previous session's record, the quality of trading was far superior: 4,704 deals were recorded, the highest in weeks, pointing to broad-based activity from retail and local institutional investors. The banking sector once again dominated volumes, and foreign selling dropped to its lowest level in over a year, confirming that local demand has fully taken the reins.

3. In Focus: Banking Sector — Volume Leader and Market Anchor

The Banks, Finance & Investment (BI) index closed at 19,528.67, a fresh cyclical high, cementing the sector's role as the primary engine of the current expansion. Two stories stood out:

NMB executed a pre-arranged block trade of 108,000 shares on top of 120,782 shares on the normal board. Total turnover for the counter exceeded TZS 3.67 billion, and the price held firm at 16,160, demonstrating deep institutional demand. With the TZS 610.15 dividend approved and the ex-dividend date expected in July, the stock continues to attract income-focused money.

CRDB was the most liquid counter, with 928,067 shares changing hands. The bid-side depth (225,734 shares) was nearly triple the offer-side (82,285), a structural demand imbalance that has supported the stock's steady grind higher. CRDB's wide branch network and mobile banking reach give it a competitive moat in a market where financial inclusion is still expanding.

KCB surged to 1,950 on volume of 336,813 shares, the highest among cross-listed counters. The move may reflect a regional convergence play as East African markets deepen their integration.

4. Today's Concept: What Is a Block Trade?

A block trade is a large, privately negotiated transaction between two parties, typically executed away from the open order book or on a pre-arranged board. It allows institutions to move significant positions without causing sharp price swings. The 108,000-share NMB block on Monday is a textbook example: the price remained stable near 16,160, while turnover surged without disrupting the equilibrium. For retail investors, block trades are a sign of deep liquidity and institutional confidence — not a reason to panic or chase.

5. Bond Market: Duration Demand, but Rotation Pressure Eases

Government bonds recorded TZS 16.20 billion in turnover, led by a 25-year bond with a 15.95% coupon that traded at prices implying yields of 10.1–11.8%. Pension funds and insurers continue to buy long-duration paper for asset-liability matching, attracted by tax-free interest. Crucially, this demand is no longer draining liquidity from equities. The rotation signal we monitored earlier in the year has given way to a more balanced market where bond and equity demand coexist.

6. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

  • Near-Term (July): The NMB ex-dividend date will be set shortly; expect pre-ex-date positioning and a post-ex-date adjustment. VODA's full-year results are imminent — a positive dividend surprise could push the stock beyond 800.
  • Medium-Term (Q3): With foreign selling at negligible levels, the market is now driven by local institutions. Watch for any Bank of Tanzania policy shift, particularly if food or fuel inflation surprises to the upside.
  • Long-Term: The structural tailwinds — urbanisation, mobile money, regional integration, and a young population — remain firmly in place. The quiet end of the foreign exit is a milestone that should not be overlooked.

Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework

The disappearance of foreign selling — after more than a year of persistent outflows — marks a structural turning point for the DSE. A market that was once at the mercy of international capital flows is now anchored by local pension funds, retail investors, and a growing domestic institutional base. This is the kind of foundational shift that multi-year allocators look for: a market that is maturing, deepening, and becoming more resilient. The banking sector, as the largest and most liquid proxy for Tanzania's economic growth, continues to be the most direct way to participate in that long-term story.
