3. In Focus: Telecoms — The Integration Catalyst

Tanzania’s telecommunications sector is no longer just about voice and data. It is the backbone of financial inclusion, a driver of regional integration, and increasingly a direct play on East Africa’s consumer story. Monday’s price action in VODA (Vodacom Tanzania) — up 25 shillings to close at TZS 770 — reflects more than a technical bounce. It signals the market beginning to price in three powerful, overlapping catalysts.

a) Mobile money as the integration engine

Vodacom’s M‑Pesa platform is the dominant mobile money service in Tanzania, with over 9 million active users. What is less appreciated is the cross‑border dimension. The interoperability agreements being rolled out across the East African Community — linking M‑Pesa with Safaricom’s Kenyan network and MTN’s platforms in Uganda and Rwanda — are turning mobile money into a regional payments rail. For VODA, this means a larger addressable market, higher transaction volumes, and a stickier customer base. Every additional cross‑border remittance or trade settlement that flows through the mobile wallet increases the company’s fee income without requiring proportionate capital expenditure.

b) The dividend catalyst

VODA’s financial year ends in March, and results are expected within days. The market anticipates a dividend proposal in the range of TZS 25–30 per share, translating to a net yield of approximately 3.2–3.9% at current prices. In an environment where the BOT benchmark rate remains low and government bond yields are compressing, a steady, growing dividend from a dominant telecom operator is highly attractive. The pre‑announcement accumulation we observed on Monday — 48,755 shares, well above the stock’s average daily volume — suggests that income‑focused investors are positioning ahead of the news.

c) Digital infrastructure and the data boom

Tanzania’s young, urbanising population is driving an insatiable demand for data. Smartphone penetration is rising, and 4G coverage is expanding beyond major cities. VODA’s continued investment in network infrastructure positions it to capture this growth. Meanwhile, the government’s push for digital financial services — including the recent integration of mobile money with government payment systems — provides a regulatory tailwind that should not be underestimated.

The broader integration theme

Telecoms are the hidden enablers of East African capital markets integration. As the DSE deepens its links with the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE) and the Uganda Securities Exchange (USE), the ability to move money seamlessly across borders becomes critical. A Tanzanian investor buying KCB shares on the DSE cross‑listing, or a Kenyan institution settling a bond trade in Dar es Salaam, relies on the payments infrastructure that companies like VODA are building. Telecoms are not merely a defensive consumer sector; they are a structural growth play on regional integration.

What to watch:

  • VODA’s upcoming results and dividend announcement. A positive surprise on earnings or a larger‑than‑expected dividend could push the stock through the psychological TZS 800 barrier.
  • Cross‑border mobile money interoperability. Any formal announcement from the EAC Secretariat or individual central banks on harmonised mobile money regulations would be a significant catalyst for the entire telecom sector.
  • Competitive dynamics. The entry of new mobile money players or regulatory changes to M‑Pesa’s dominance (e.g., mandatory interoperability with bank wallets) could alter the landscape. For now, VODA’s network effect remains a formidable moat.

4. Bond Market: Duration Demand Persists, but the Panic Is Over

Government bonds traded TZS 16.20 billion, dominated by the 25‑year 15.95% bond at prices ranging from 118.63% to 142.36% of face value (yields ~10.1–11.8%). The steep yield curve remains attractive for institutional buyers, but the competitive frenzy that drove the rotation out of equities earlier in 2025 has clearly subsided. With foreign selling in equities now negligible, the bond market is no longer a threat to equity liquidity — it is a complementary, income‑focused allocation.

For bond investors: The current environment favours a barbell strategy — short‑term paper for liquidity and long‑duration bonds for tax‑effective income, while remaining underweight the belly of the curve where inflation risk is most acute.

5. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

Near‑Term (Weeks 1–4)

  • Telecom results season: VODA’s announcement is the most immediate catalyst. A positive outcome could lift the entire Commercial Services index.
  • NMB dividend: The ex‑dividend date for NMB’s TZS 610.15 per share dividend is expected in July. Expect pre‑ex‑date positioning.
  • Banking consolidation: The BI index at 19,528.67 is at a cyclical high. Profit‑taking is normal and healthy.

Medium‑Term (3–6 months)

  • Regional integration milestones: Progress on the EAC single market for financial services would benefit cross‑listed stocks (KCB, NMG) and telecoms (VODA).
  • BOT policy: A stable interest rate environment supports both equity valuations and bond market stability. Watch for any shift in rhetoric on inflation.
  • Fiscal policy: The new fiscal year begins in July. Infrastructure spending plans will directly impact cement, construction, and logistics names.

Long‑Term (1–5 years)

The mobile money revolution is still in its early innings. As interoperability expands and digital payments displace cash, telecom companies with strong mobile money platforms will be among the primary beneficiaries — not just in Tanzania, but across the EAC. This is a multi‑decade structural trend that aligns perfectly with Amana Capital EA’s long‑horizon philosophy.
