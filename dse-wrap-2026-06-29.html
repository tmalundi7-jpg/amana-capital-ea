Amana Capital East Africa — Daily Market Intelligence

Monday, 29th June 2026 | Closing Analysis & Strategic Outlook

The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange opened the week with a session that, at first glance, appeared quiet: equity turnover of TZS 7.09 billion was a fraction of Friday’s record TZS 82.7 billion. But a deeper reading reveals a market that is quietly strengthening its foundations. The All Share Index (DSEI) added 14.93 points to close at 4,040.04, a level last seen in early June. The real story, however, lies beneath the headline numbers — in the surge of deals, the near‑extinction of foreign selling, and the unmistakable message from the bond market.

1. Equity Market: A Picture of Broad, Domestic Strength

Turnover and Participation

While equity turnover fell by 91% from the previous session’s anomalous block‑trade‑driven spike, it remains comfortably above the TZS 3 billion threshold that separates an active market from a sleepy one. More importantly, the number of deals jumped to 4,704, the highest in weeks. This combination — moderate turnover with a high deal count — is the hallmark of broad‑based participation. Rather than a single institution moving a large block and dominating the tape, Monday’s market was characterised by thousands of smaller orders from retail investors, local pension funds, and proprietary desks. This is a healthy, domestically‑anchored market structure.

Indices and Sector Leadership

The Banking, Finance & Investment (BI) index closed at 19,528.67, a fresh cyclical high, confirming that banks remain the primary engine of the current expansion. The Industrial & Allied (IA) index was essentially flat, while Commercial Services (CS) edged higher. The leadership rotation is consistent with a mid‑cycle phase: financials typically lead when credit growth is robust and interest rates are stable, while industrials and consumer cyclicals often join the rally later as capacity utilisation tightens.

Top Movers — Where the Action Was

  • MKCB (+5.77%) and MBP (+5.24%) posted the largest percentage gains, though both moved on very thin volumes (2,274 and 2,405 shares respectively). These are small‑cap, illiquid counters, and such moves should be interpreted with caution — a single order can swing the price by several percentage points. Investors navigating these names are urged to read our guide on Navigating DSE Liquidity.
  • VODA (+3.36%) and TOL (+2.11%) provided more meaningful signals. VODA’s 48,755‑share volume and push toward TZS 770 suggest the market is beginning to price in the upcoming dividend announcement, a catalyst we have been monitoring. TOL’s rise on 6,605 shares reflects a steady bid for an oil marketing company that benefits from stable fuel demand and a manageable import cost structure.
  • CRDB (+0.39%) remained the most liquid counter with 928,067 shares traded. The bid‑side depth (225,734 shares at the close) was nearly triple the offer‑side (82,285), indicating persistent demand. This accumulation pattern, coupled with the bank’s wide branch network and strong capital position, reinforces the investment case for long‑horizon allocators — though valuation remains a critical check.
  • NMB (+0.31%) traded 120,782 shares on the normal board and a pre‑arranged block of 108,000 shares. The block, executed without disrupting the market price, underscores the depth of demand at the TZS 16,160 level. NMB’s market capitalisation now exceeds TZS 8 trillion, by far the largest on the DSE. The pending dividend of TZS 610.15 per share (ex‑dividend date expected in July) provides a near‑term anchor.

Losers — Liquidity Risk Illustrated

MCB (-4.06%) and TTP (-4.00%) were the notable decliners. Both are low‑volume counters where a single sell order can push the price down sharply. This is a practical reminder that illiquidity is itself a risk factor that must be priced into any investment decision. A stock appearing “cheap” on a P/E basis may simply be offering a discount for its illiquidity.

Foreign Flow — The Handoff Is Complete

Perhaps the most significant data point of the day is the foreign flow. Foreign buying contributed a negligible 0.34% of turnover, while foreign selling was only 3.65% — the lowest print in over a year. To put this in perspective, foreign selling averaged 53.35% of turnover in the April–June 2025 quarter and 42.04% in the first quarter of 2026. The steady decline, culminating in Monday’s figure, indicates that the sustained foreign exodus that weighed on the DSE for much of 2025 has now fully reversed. Local demand is now the sole driver of market direction.

Implication: The market’s fate is now firmly in local hands. This is both an opportunity and a responsibility. Local institutional investors — pension funds, unit trusts, insurance companies — have the liquidity to sustain the expansion, provided economic fundamentals remain supportive. The near‑term risk is not foreign selling, but domestic complacency.

2. Bond Market: Duration Demand Remains, but the Urgency Has Eased

Government bonds recorded a turnover of TZS 16.20 billion, down from TZS 48.71 billion on Friday. The standout trade was a 25‑year bond with a 15.95% coupon (face value TZS 15.04 billion) that changed hands at prices ranging from 118.63% to 142.36% of face value. These prices imply a yield‑to‑maturity of approximately 10.1% to 11.8% — still highly attractive for long‑term institutional portfolios, but no longer at the extreme levels that drove the rapid rotation into bonds earlier in the year.

A 20‑year bond with a 15.25% coupon traded at 145.63% of face, and a 10‑year bond with a 14.00% coupon traded at 120.00%. The yield curve remains steep by regional standards, compensating long‑duration investors for inflation and currency risk.

Corporate Bonds: A 5‑year corporate bond with a 12.00% coupon traded at 96.41% of face value, implying a yield above the coupon. Corporate credit risk is still priced conservatively, but the small deal size (TZS 200 million face) limits its signal value.

What the Bond Market Is Telling Us: The bond market is no longer draining liquidity from equities. The rotation signal — which we tracked closely when bond turnover surged and equity foreign selling spiked — has moved into a post‑handoff phase. Institutions are still buying duration, but the frenzied competition for long‑dated paper has eased. This is a benign backdrop for equities: bond yields remain attractive enough to anchor institutional portfolios, but not so volatile as to trigger sharp reallocations.

  • Long‑duration bonds (15‑25 years) continue to offer tax‑effective income of 10–15% p.a. for Tanzanian residents, depending on entry price.
  • The primary auction calendar should be watched. Any surprise increase in issuance could push yields higher, creating capital losses for existing bondholders.
  • Short‑term bonds and Treasury bills (under 2 years) provide a near‑cash alternative with negligible capital risk, suitable for the 10% cash allocation we advocate in portfolio construction.

3. ETF Market: Steady but Not Yet a Core Allocation

The IEACLC‑ETF traded 354,823 shares at TZS 1,250, while the VERTEX‑ETF traded 104,839 shares at TZS 335. Foreign participation was minimal. ETFs remain a small but growing segment of the DSE. For retail investors seeking instant diversification without the complexity of individual stock selection, these instruments are worth understanding. However, liquidity in the ETFs themselves is moderate, and spreads should be checked before placing orders.

4. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

Near‑Term (Weeks 1–4)

  • NMB Dividend: The ex‑dividend date for NMB’s TZS 610.15 per share dividend is expected in July. Historically, stocks tend to trade up into the ex‑date as income‑focused investors position themselves, then adjust downward by approximately the dividend amount post‑ex‑date.
  • VODA Announcement: The market is anticipating VODA’s full‑year results and dividend proposal. A positive surprise could push the stock through the TZS 800 level.
  • Banking Sector Momentum: With the BI index at all‑time highs, a period of consolidation would be normal and not a trend reversal.

Medium‑Term (3–6 months)

  • Interest Rate Environment: Any signal of a BOT rate hike would be the most significant macro event for the market.
  • Budget and Fiscal Policy: The new fiscal year begins in July. Cement and construction names are particularly sensitive to public spending plans.
  • Foreign Flow Re‑Entry? A shift from 0.34% buying to even 5–10% would be a powerful catalyst for the DSEI.

Long‑Term (1–5 years)

The structural drivers — urbanisation, financial inclusion, regional integration, infrastructure spending, and a young population — remain intact. The Amana Capital philosophy of long‑horizon, disciplined conviction continues to be the appropriate framework for wealth creation on the DSE.
