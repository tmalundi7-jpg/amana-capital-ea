Two-Day Analysis: Tuesday 30 June & Wednesday 1 July 2026

Closing Analysis, Educational Insight & Multi-Year Perspective

The transition from June into the new quarter brought a decisive change in market character. Tuesday’s session was steady but unremarkable; Wednesday’s session was the most encouraging day for the DSE in over a month. The return of foreign buying, a broad-based surge in deal activity, and the banking sector’s push to a fresh cycle high combined to lift the All Share Index to 4,057.22. This report examines the data, explains the mechanics of the DSE’s 5% price bands, and highlights the long-term implications of the shift in market leadership.

Tuesday was a normal liquidity day with TZS 2.97 billion in equity turnover and heavy bond activity of over TZS 30 billion. Wednesday’s turnover vaulted past the TZS 5 billion mark — a clear high-regime session — driven by thousands of individual orders rather than a single block trade. The number of deals climbed to 3,522, the second highest reading in weeks, indicating that retail investors, local institutions, and the returning foreign bid are all contributing to market depth.

2. The Return of Foreign Capital

The single most important development of the two days was the reversal in foreign investor behaviour. On Tuesday, foreign selling still outweighed buying by a wide margin (7.26% of turnover vs. negligible buying). By Wednesday, foreign buying surged to 11.43% of total equity turnover — approximately TZS 588 million — while selling contracted to 5.88%.

The day produced a net foreign inflow of around TZS 285 million, the first meaningful daily net buying since early June. This change likely reflects a combination of stabilising global risk appetite, a Tanzanian shilling that has held steady near 2,625 to the US dollar, and equity valuations that became more attractive after the mid-June correction.

For local investors, the return of foreign capital is doubly important: it removes a persistent source of selling pressure and can catalyse a broader re-rating of the large-cap stocks that dominate the DSE. The foreign-exit narrative that defined the first half of 2026 now appears to be behind us.

4. In Focus: CRDB — A Rally with Fundamentals and Flow Behind It

CRDB’s move from 2,630 to 2,730 on Wednesday was significant not just for its size but for the quality of the trading behind it. Over 507,000 shares changed hands across 1,793 deals, and the stock touched an intraday high of 2,760 before settling near the top of its range. The bid-side order book was deep, and the ratio of outstanding bids to offers remained heavily skewed toward buying interest.

Three factors aligned to drive the advance. First, the return of foreign buying provided a marginal bid that had been absent for weeks. Second, CRDB’s valuation — a price-to-earnings ratio around 10.4 based on trailing earnings, and a price-to-book ratio near 1.8 — remained reasonable in the context of mid-teen returns on equity and a growing loan book. Third, the broader banking sector narrative of rising net interest margins, stable asset quality, and expanding mobile banking channels continues to attract both local institutions and returning international investors.

5. Today’s Concept: What Is a 5% Daily Price Band and Why Does It Matter?

Every listed stock on the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange is subject to a daily price movement limit. For most actively traded equities, this limit is ±5% of the previous day’s closing price. Some smaller or less liquid counters have a wider band, typically ±15%. The rule is designed to prevent extreme intraday swings and to give the market time to absorb new information without disorderly trading.

On Wednesday, CRDB closed at 2,630 on Tuesday. The next day’s permissible range was therefore:

  • Upper limit: 2,630 × 1.05 = 2,761.50
  • Lower limit: 2,630 × 0.95 = 2,498.50

CRDB’s intraday high of 2,760 fell just below the upper cap. Any buy order submitted at 2,770 would have been rejected by the exchange’s trading system because it was outside the allowed range. This mechanism directly affects investors who use limit orders. If you place a buy limit above the 5% ceiling, it will not execute until the stock’s closing price rises sufficiently to lift the following day’s band.

6. Bond Market: Duration Demand Persists, Rotation Pressure Eases

Government bond trading remained active, with combined two-day turnover of TZS 52.16 billion. The 25-year bonds with coupons of 13.25% and 15.95% were the most heavily traded instruments, changing hands at yields around 10.6%–11.3%. Pension funds and insurance companies continue to purchase long-duration paper for asset-liability matching, attracted by the tax-free status of government bond interest.

Importantly, the bond market is no longer draining liquidity from equities. Throughout June, we tracked a rotation signal where surging bond turnover coincided with heavy foreign equity selling. With foreign selling now collapsing and buying returning, that rotation is in a post-handoff phase.

7. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate Near Term (July 2026)

  • NMB ex-dividend date: The AGM has approved the TZS 610.15 combined dividend. The ex-dividend date will be set shortly. Historically, stocks tend to rally into the ex-date as income investors position themselves, then adjust downward by approximately the dividend amount after the entitlement is detached.
  • VODA FY2025 results: Expected within days. The market is pricing in a dividend proposal in the range of TZS 25–30 per share.
  • Foreign flow trend: If foreign buying remains above 10% of turnover for three consecutive sessions, it would confirm a structural shift in market participation and could trigger a broader re-rating of the DSEI toward the 4,100 level.

Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework

The two-day data tells a story that transcends the daily fluctuations: the foreign exit that defined the DSE for much of 2025 and early 2026 is over. Foreign capital is tentatively returning, and the banking sector — the largest and most liquid part of the market — is leading the advance. For an allocator with a multi-year horizon, these are the signals that matter. Discipline, not timing, remains the foundation of long-term investment success.
