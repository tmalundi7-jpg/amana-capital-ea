Daily DSE Wrap | Friday, 6th July 2026

DSEI Surges Past 4,100 as Banking Sector and Block Trades Drive Rally

The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange capped the week with a powerful broad-based rally, pushing the All-Share Index firmly through the 4,100 barrier for the first time since early June. Heavy block trades in NMB and the IEACLC ETF, combined with strong demand for CRDB and VODA, lifted turnover to its highest level of the week. The market is no longer simply recovering — it is accelerating, and it is doing so on the back of deep, institutional-quality liquidity.

The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange kicked off the new trading week with its highest turnover in nearly two weeks, as a combination of large block trades and broad retail participation pushed total equity turnover to TZS 9.21 billion. While the All-Share Index eased marginally from its recent peak, the underlying activity — 4,781 deals and over 1.65 million shares traded — signalled strong, domestically-driven conviction. This report decodes the day's data, explains how the DSE's 5% daily price bands shape trading, and frames the long-term implications of a market that is now firmly in local hands.

Turnover surged into the "high" regime, comfortably above the TZS 3 billion threshold. The surge was driven by two pre-arranged block trades — 229,000 shares of NMB and 400,000 shares of TCCL — but the jump in deals to 4,781 confirmed that thousands of smaller retail and institutional orders also flowed through the market.

Foreign flow remained muted: buying at 0.14% and selling at 6.70%. Local pension funds, unit trusts, and retail investors are absorbing all available supply, a structural shift that has profound implications for market stability.

NMB continued its march toward the TZS 17,000 mark. VODA added another 1.9% as the market positions ahead of the imminent FY2025 dividend announcement. CRDB pulled back 1.1% to TZS 2,770 after Friday's close at 2,800 — a healthy pause that brought the stock back within striking distance of key support levels.

3. In Focus: The 5% Daily Price Band

Every stock listed on the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange is subject to a maximum daily price movement limit. For most actively traded equities, this limit is ±5% from the previous trading day's closing price. A handful of smaller, less liquid counters are permitted a wider band of ±15%. The purpose is to prevent extreme intraday volatility and to give the market time to digest new information in an orderly fashion.

On Monday, CRDB traded between 2,740 and 2,800 — well within the band. No orders outside that range would have been accepted by the exchange's automated trading system.

4. Bond Market: A Complementary Expansion

Government bond turnover rose to TZS 33.03 billion, led by heavy trading in 20-year paper (the 15.49% coupon bond was especially active) and the 25-year 13.25% bond. Yields on these long-dated instruments hovered around 10.5-11.5%, still attractive for pension funds and insurance companies seeking tax-free, long-duration assets.

Crucially, the bond market is no longer a competitor to equities; it is a complement. Bond turnover and equity turnover rose together on Monday, indicating that institutional money is expanding into both asset classes simultaneously.

5. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

  • Near-Term (July): VODA's FY2025 dividend announcement is expected imminently. NMB's ex-dividend date has yet to be published but should appear shortly. CRDB consolidation around 2,740-2,770 sets a solid base for the next leg higher.
  • Medium-Term (Q3 2026): The Bank of Tanzania's monetary policy stance remains accommodative. Any signal of a rate change would be the single most important macro event for both equities and bonds.
  • Long-Term: The foreign-exit handoff is complete. A market that was once highly sensitive to international capital flows is now anchored by domestic pension funds, a growing retail base, and diaspora participation.

6. Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework

The combination of surging turnover, broad-based deal activity, and a collapsed foreign-selling share is telling a quiet but powerful story: the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange is maturing. A market that once lurched between foreign inflows and outflows is now driven by local institutions and retail investors who understand the long-term value of Tanzanian enterprises. Patience and discipline remain the investor's greatest assets.
