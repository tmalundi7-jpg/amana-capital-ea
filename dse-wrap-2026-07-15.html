Daily DSE Wrap | Wednesday, 15th July 2026

Market Returns to Calm After Block Trades; Foreign Selling Drops to Historic Low

Wednesday’s session on the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange felt like the quiet after a storm. After two weeks of record-breaking block trades and dramatic shifts in ownership, the market settled into a steady, unhurried rhythm. Turnover dropped back to a normal TZS 2.71 billion, but the number of deals actually rose to 2,913, showing that everyday investors were actively participating. Most importantly, foreign selling fell to a mere 0.78% of turnover — the lowest level in over a year — confirming that the prolonged exit of international funds is now truly behind us.

Equity turnover fell sharply from the previous day’s block-trade-inflated figure, but this was entirely expected. The normal board activity was healthy, with CRDB trading over 440,000 shares, VODA over 63,000, and NMB over 42,000 (plus a block of 31,549 shares). The All-Share Index gained 11 points, recovering to its highest level in more than a week.

Foreign participation was almost invisible: buying at 0.68% and selling at 0.78% — both negligible. The foreign funds that had been selling since May have now fully exited their large positions. The market is now driven by local pension funds, retail investors, and institutional buyers who are holding for the long term.

Bond trading remained strong at TZS 42.8 billion, led by the new 20-year 12.25% bond and high-coupon 25-year paper. The fact that both equities and bonds are attracting deep local liquidity at the same time is a powerful sign of a maturing financial market.

3. Today’s Concept: How the 5% Daily Price Band Protects You and Shapes Your Trading

On Wednesday, CRDB closed at 2,690. That means that on Thursday, the stock cannot be traded at just any price — it must remain within a band of ±5% around that closing level. For CRDB, the limits are:

  • Upper limit: 2,690 × 1.05 = 2,824.50
  • Lower limit: 2,690 × 0.95 = 2,555.50

The same rule applies to almost all actively traded stocks on the DSE. Here are the bands for a selection of widely held counters, based on Wednesday’s closing prices:

Why does this rule exist?

The 5% daily price band is a safety mechanism. It prevents a single large order or a moment of panic from causing a stock price to collapse or surge wildly in a single session. It gives the market time to absorb news and for orderly trading to take place. If you try to place a buy order above the upper limit or a sell order below the lower limit, the exchange’s automated system will reject it.

How to use it:

Before you set a limit order, always check the previous day’s closing price and calculate the allowed range. This ensures your order is executable from the moment you place it. During the recent block trades in CRDB, for example, the price did not swing outside its band precisely because the mechanism worked as designed.

4. Bond Market: Local Institutions Lock In Tax-Free Income

Government bond turnover remained elevated at TZS 42.8 billion. The busiest instrument was the new 20-year bond with a 12.25% coupon, which traded in massive volume at prices ranging from 100% to 112% of face value — yields of approximately 10.8% to 11.6%, entirely tax-free. Pension funds and insurance companies continue to buy long-dated paper, and the simultaneous strength in both bonds and equities shows that local institutions have plenty of firepower for both asset classes.

For individual investors, bonds are accessible through unit trusts like the UTT AMIS Bond Fund, offering a liquid, diversified way to earn returns that can outpace inflation.

5. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

Near-Term (July):

  • With the CRDB block trades now absorbed, the market is likely to trade in a calmer, fundamentals-driven range. Foreign selling is no longer a headwind.
  • VODA’s full-year results and dividend proposal are expected any day. A strong announcement could push the stock toward the upper end of its 5% band.
  • NMB’s ex-dividend date has not yet been published but is imminent. The stock is at an all-time high; the historical pattern of pre-ex-date strength followed by a post-ex-date adjustment remains a useful context for income-focused investors.

Medium-Term (Q3 2026):

  • The Bank of Tanzania’s steady policy stance remains supportive. Any change in the benchmark interest rate would be the most significant macro event for both equities and bonds.
  • Government infrastructure spending will directly affect cement, construction, and logistics companies.

Long-Term:

  • The events of the past two weeks — the historic transfer of CRDB shares from foreign to local hands — represent a landmark in the DSE’s development. A market anchored by patient, domestic institutions is more stable, more predictable, and better able to reflect the genuine, long-term growth of Tanzania’s economy.

7. Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework

The quiet, almost uneventful nature of Wednesday’s trading is precisely the kind of market that long-term investors should welcome. After weeks of dramatic block trades and the final exit of foreign sellers, the DSE is settling into a new equilibrium — one where local buyers set the prices, where daily volatility is manageable, and where a stock’s true, underlying value has the space to reveal itself. This stability, not the thrilling spikes or the frightening dips, is the environment in which patient wealth is built.
