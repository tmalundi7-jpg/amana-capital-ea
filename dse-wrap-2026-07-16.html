Daily DSE Wrap | Thursday, 16th July 2026

Third CRDB Block Seals the Handover as Foreign Buyers Begin to Return

Thursday’s session at the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange was yet another chapter in the historic transfer of CRDB shares that has defined July. A third enormous block trade — 18.3 million CRDB shares — drove total equity turnover to TZS 55 billion, but the more important signal was the quiet return of foreign buying. For the first time in weeks, international investors were net purchasers on the normal board, a tentative sign that the market’s newfound stability is beginning to attract capital back from overseas.

Equity turnover was once again dominated by a single pre-arranged block trade — 18,329,324 shares of CRDB, worth approximately TZS 49 billion. Normal-board activity remained healthy, with over 733,000 CRDB shares traded across 1,489 deals, showing that small and mid-sized investors continue to participate actively. The All-Share Index edged higher, closing at its best level in two weeks.

Foreign investor activity told an encouraging story: foreign buying jumped to 4.42% of turnover (TZS 2.44 billion), the highest percentage in weeks and the largest absolute daily purchase since early June. While foreign selling at 7.19% still resulted in a small net outflow, the return of meaningful buying interest after a prolonged absence suggests that international capital is beginning to take note of the clean shareholder register and attractive valuations now that the large-scale foreign exit has been completed.

CRDB held perfectly steady at TZS 2,690, the same price it has closed at for three consecutive sessions despite over 100 million shares changing hands in block trades this month. VODA slipped slightly to 800, though the volume remained light; the market is clearly waiting for the company’s dividend announcement.

3. In Focus: What the Third CRDB Block Trade Tells Us

Thursday’s 18.3 million-share CRDB trade is the third massive block in July, following 58 million shares on the 10th and 26.8 million on the 14th. Together, more than 103 million CRDB shares have been transferred in a single month — a substantial fraction of the bank’s publicly traded stock. This is not a coincidence; it is the final stage of a carefully orchestrated ownership transfer.

A single large foreign institution (or possibly a group of institutions) has been methodically selling its stake. On the other side, one or more local institutions — most likely pension funds or a strategic consortium — have been absorbing the shares at a steady price of around 2,690. By executing these trades on the pre-arranged block market, both parties avoid disrupting the normal board, protecting everyday investors from sudden price shocks.

What does this mean for ordinary investors?

First, the persistent seller is now almost certainly out. The overhang that has kept CRDB’s price range-bound for weeks has been lifted. Second, the new owners are patient, multi-year holders who have no incentive to sell at these levels. Third, the return of foreign buying on the normal board — not the block market — suggests that other international investors, seeing the clean shareholder register, are beginning to dip their toes back in. For the long-term investor in CRDB, these are all positive structural changes.

4. Today’s Concept: The 5% Daily Price Band — Your Safety Net

On Thursday, CRDB closed at 2,690. That means on Friday, the stock cannot trade freely — it must remain within a band of ±5% around that closing price. The limits are:

  • Upper limit: 2,690 × 1.05 = 2,824.50
  • Lower limit: 2,690 × 0.95 = 2,555.50

Any order to buy above 2,824.50 or to sell below 2,555.50 will be automatically rejected by the DSE’s trading system. Here are the bands for other widely held stocks, based on Thursday’s closing prices:

The band acts as a safety net, preventing panic-driven swings and giving the market time to absorb large transactions in an orderly way. Before setting any limit order, always check the previous day’s close and calculate the allowed range — this small habit ensures your order is executable from the moment you place it.

5. Bond Market: Steady Demand for Long-Term, Tax-Free Income

Government bond turnover remained elevated at TZS 42.4 billion, with the busiest trading in the new 20-year 12.25% bond (TZS 13.1 billion face value) and the 25-year 15.95% bond (TZS 1.99 billion). Yields on these instruments settled around 10.7% to 11.6%, entirely free of withholding tax. Pension funds and insurance companies continue to buy long-dated paper, and the simultaneous strength in both bonds and equities confirms that local institutions have ample liquidity to support the entire market.

For individual investors, bonds are accessible through unit trusts such as the UTT AMIS Bond Fund, offering a diversified, low-effort way to earn returns that comfortably outpace inflation.

6. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

Near-Term (July):

  • The CRDB block trades appear to be approaching their conclusion. Once the overhang is fully absorbed, the stock may begin to reflect its underlying fundamentals, which remain strong.
  • VODA’s full-year results and dividend proposal are overdue and could be announced at any time. A positive surprise may trigger a rapid move toward the stock’s upper 5% band.
  • NMB’s ex-dividend date has still not been published but is imminent. The stock is at an all-time high, and the historical pattern of pre-ex-date strength followed by a post-ex-date adjustment continues to be a useful framework for income-focused investors.

Medium-Term (Q3 2026):

  • The Bank of Tanzania’s monetary policy remains supportive. Any change in the benchmark interest rate would be the most significant macroeconomic event for both equities and bonds.
  • The implementation of the new fiscal year’s infrastructure spending will directly affect cement, construction, and logistics companies.

Long-Term:

  • The events of July — the transfer of over 100 million CRDB shares from foreign to local hands, the stabilisation of the market, and the tentative return of international buying — mark a milestone in the DSE’s development. A market that is anchored by patient, domestic institutions is more stable, more predictable, and better able to reflect the genuine, long-term growth of Tanzania’s economy.

8. Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework

The third CRDB block of the month, combined with the quiet return of foreign buyers, marks the end of a cycle that has reshaped the DSE. Over 100 million shares have moved from international funds into patient, local hands that are aligned with Tanzania’s long-term prosperity. For a multi-year investor, the removal of a persistent seller and the emergence of a stable, domestically anchored shareholder base are far more important than any single day’s price movement. The foundations of the market are now stronger, and the stage is set for the next chapter of East African capital markets.
