Daily DSE Wrap | Thursday, 23rd July 2026

Foreign Capital Returns as VODA Surges and CRDB Block Activity Resumes

The Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange extended its gains on Thursday, with the All-Share Index closing at a fresh recent high. A renewed wave of institutional block trades and a sharp jump in VODA provided the day’s main talking points, while the even split between foreign buying and selling confirmed that international capital is once again active in the market — not as a net seller fleeing, but as a two-way participant.

1. Market Snapshot

Turnover eased from Wednesday’s extraordinary spike but remained comfortably above the TZS 3 billion mark that signals a liquid, active market. Volume surged because of three pre-arranged block trades: one million shares of AFRIPRISE, nearly one million shares of CRDB, and over 186,000 shares of TCCL. These blocks accounted for a large portion of the day’s activity, while normal-board trading continued at a steady pace.

The foreign flow figures told a particularly encouraging story: foreign buying and selling each accounted for exactly 46% of turnover. Net flows were neutral, but the sheer volume shows that international institutions are now trading actively in both directions. This is a fundamentally different environment from the one-sided foreign selling that dominated the first half of the year.

VODA was the day’s standout, rallying 2.5% to 820 and touching an intraday high of 840. The stock is clearly being lifted by anticipation of the company’s full-year results and dividend proposal. CRDB inched up to 2,690, continuing its slow, steady climb out of the consolidation range that followed July’s enormous block transfers. TBL built on Wednesday’s foreign-driven block trade to reach 10,170, a new level that reflects its reliable dividend appeal.

3. In Focus: The Return of Two-Way Foreign Flow

For months, the dominant narrative on the DSE was the persistent exit of foreign investors. In June, foreign selling often exceeded 40% of turnover. In July, we saw that selling collapse to near zero. Now, in the last two sessions, foreign activity has surged back — but this time it is balanced. On Thursday, foreign buying and selling each accounted for 46% of turnover, the mirror image of a healthy, two-sided market.

This matters for everyday investors. When global institutions are both buying and selling, they bring liquidity, narrow the gap between bid and ask prices, and signal that they consider the market fairly valued. The large CRDB block trade — nearly one million shares — was almost certainly part of this foreign activity, yet the price moved only 10 shillings. That stability is a direct result of deep local demand absorbing the flow.

A market that can handle million-share blocks without drama is a market that is maturing. It is also a market that offers better conditions for all investors, whether they trade a few dozen shares or a few thousand.

4. Today’s Concept: The 5% Daily Price Band — a Safety Net in Action

Thursday’s CRDB block trade moved nearly one million shares, yet the price barely budged. Part of the reason is the exchange rule that limits how far any stock can move in a single day. For most actively traded companies, this daily price band is ±5% of the previous day’s closing price.

Based on Thursday’s closing prices, here are the allowed trading ranges for Friday, 24 July 2026:

If you are planning to buy or sell any of these stocks on Friday, your limit price must fall within the range shown. An order placed outside the band will be automatically rejected by the DSE’s trading system. The band is not a restriction designed to frustrate; it is a protective measure that ensures orderly trading, even when large institutional blocks are being executed.

5. Bond Market: Long-Term Yields Remain Attractive

Government bond turnover recovered to TZS 24.6 billion, with the busiest trading in the 15-year 10.75% bond and the 20-year 12.25% bond. Yields on these instruments ranged from approximately 10.0% to 11.7%, entirely free of withholding tax. The bond market continues to offer a deep, liquid alternative for local institutions seeking predictable, long-term income.

6. Strategic Outlook: What to Anticipate

Near-Term (Late July):
The VODA rally to 840 suggests that the market is pricing in a positive dividend announcement. When the company’s full-year results are released, there could be further movement within the 5% daily band.
The reappearance of a large CRDB block trade, coupled with balanced foreign flow, indicates that the post-handover market is functioning smoothly. The stock is gradually edging higher as local demand continues to absorb any institutional sales.
NMB remains anchored near its all-time high. The ex-dividend date is still pending; the stock’s behaviour after the books close will be an important signal.

Medium-Term (Q3 2026):
The Bank of Tanzania’s policy stance remains supportive. Any change in the benchmark interest rate would be the most significant macro event for both equities and bonds.
The implementation of the government’s infrastructure budget will directly affect cement, construction, and logistics companies.

Long-Term:
The return of active, two-way foreign participation — not as fleeing sellers, but as balanced traders — is a sign that the DSE is regaining its place on the radar of global portfolio managers. For the patient, long-term investor, a market that is liquid, stable, and anchored by domestic institutions is a far stronger platform for wealth creation than one that lurches between foreign inflows and outflows.

8. Considerations for a Multi-Year Framework

The sight of foreign institutions buying and selling in equal measure — and doing so at volumes that move nearly a million shares without disturbing the price — is a powerful signal. The market has absorbed the largest ownership transfer in its history, and the result is a deeper, calmer, more resilient exchange. For the multi-year investor, this maturing of the DSE’s structure is far more significant than any single day’s index level.
